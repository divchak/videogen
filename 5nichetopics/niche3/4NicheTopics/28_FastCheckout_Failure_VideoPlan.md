## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~950 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Fast raised $120 million to build a one-click checkout button. One button. To let you skip filling in your address and payment details on a website. By the time the company shut down in April 2022, it had generated approximately $600,000 in total lifetime revenue — while spending $10 million per month. The math: the company spent nearly 200 times its entire revenue in a single month of operations. And somehow, the investors who lost that $120 million were not nobodies — they included Stripe, the most sophisticated payments company on earth. So what happened?"

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Fast was founded in 2019 by Domm Holland, a young Australian entrepreneur. His insight was real: online checkout is painful. Creating an account, re-entering your address, entering your card number — it adds friction and causes cart abandonment. Billions of dollars in e-commerce transactions are lost every year because someone got annoyed during checkout.

Fast's solution: a universal login system that remembers your payment and shipping information across any participating merchant. One click, any store, done. It was conceptually similar to what Shopify's Shop Pay had built, what Apple Pay had built, and what PayPal's one-click checkout had built — and all three of those were backed by trillion-dollar platforms that already had enormous installed bases of both consumers and merchants. Fast raised $20 million in seed funding in 2020 and then $102 million more in early 2021, with Stripe leading the round."

**[THE SPENDING CULTURE — 1:30–2:30]**
"With $120 million in the bank, Fast built an opulent San Francisco office. It paid extremely high salaries and generous benefits. It sponsored events. At its peak, the company had around 400 employees — for a product that had not yet achieved anything close to commercial traction. The assumption baked into every decision: the product will scale, the revenue will come, and the current burn rate will be justified retrospectively.

Former employees described a culture where spending was celebrated rather than scrutinized. Management hired aggressively, expanded team structures, and focused on growth narratives rather than revenue metrics. The single question that was not asked with sufficient urgency: why would merchants choose Fast over Stripe's own checkout, Apple Pay, Shop Pay, and PayPal — all of which already served enormous numbers of consumers across enormous numbers of merchants?"

**[THE REVENUE REALITY — 2:30–3:15]**
"When Fast shut down in April 2022, the financial picture that emerged was stark even by the standards of money-losing startups. Total lifetime revenue: approximately $600,000. Monthly burn rate: approximately $10 million. The company had been spending at a rate that would require revenues of $120 million per year just to break even — and it was generating revenues that barely registered in the tens of thousands of dollars per month.

The underlying structural problem: Fast needed merchants to integrate its button to attract consumers. Consumers needed to encounter Fast at many merchants to justify installing it. Neither side of that two-sided marketplace ever reached critical mass. Without a critical mass of merchant integration, consumers had no reason to adopt Fast. Without a critical mass of consumer adoption, merchants had no incentive to integrate it."

**[THE SHUTDOWN — 3:15–4:00]**
"In late March 2022, The Information reported that Fast was running out of money and had approached investors for emergency capital. No investors came. On April 5, 2022, Fast announced it was shutting down. Employees were informed with minimal warning. The company cited inability to raise additional capital as the reason.

The Stripe investment deserves particular scrutiny: Stripe Checkout was a direct competitor to Fast. Stripe had invested in a company building a product that competed with Stripe's own checkout solution. Whether that represented a hedge, an intelligence-gathering exercise, or simply the kind of investment that looks obvious in 2020 but regrettable in 2022 — it has become one of Silicon Valley's more discussed strategic curiosities."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"Fast is a masterclass in the difference between fundraising success and business validation. Raising $120 million from credible investors is genuinely hard. It reflects real persuasiveness, real storytelling ability, and real investor belief. But it does not validate that the product has solved the chicken-and-egg problem of a two-sided marketplace, or that merchants will integrate it, or that consumers will use it.

The $10 million monthly burn rate was the real tell: that level of spending, before product-market fit, is not an investment in growth. It's a bet that growth will come before the money runs out. For Fast, it didn't."

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"$120 million raised. $600,000 earned. $10 million burned per month. One checkout button. Subscribe — we document every expensive lesson in startup history so you don't have to pay for it personally."
