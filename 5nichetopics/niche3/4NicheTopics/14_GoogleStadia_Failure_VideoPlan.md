## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,000 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In January 2023, Google sent a message to Stadia users: the service is shutting down in two weeks. Not months. Weeks. People who had spent thousands of dollars building a game library on the platform were getting their money back — but the games, the progress, the saves, the communities — gone. Google had promised to reinvent gaming. Instead, it delivered a textbook lesson in why reputation matters, why developers don't trust serial product killers, and why cloud gaming is harder than a press conference makes it look."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Google Stadia was announced at GDC — the Game Developers Conference — in March 2019, and the demo was genuinely impressive. The pitch: no console, no downloads, no waiting. Click a link, play a AAA game instantly on any screen, anywhere — streamed from Google's data centers in glorious 4K at 60 frames per second. Google's infrastructure had powered YouTube for billions of users. Surely it could power gaming.

Stadia launched in November 2019 with a $130 hardware bundle — a custom controller and a Chromecast Ultra. The subscription was $9.99 a month for 4K access, with a free tier also available. The technology wasn't bad — it genuinely worked under the right conditions. The problems were everywhere else."

**[THE PROBLEMS — 1:30–2:30]**
"Problem one: latency. Gaming is uniquely unforgiving of input lag. The tiny delay between pressing a button and seeing the result — imperceptible in video streaming — is deeply noticeable when you're trying to aim at something in a competitive game. Even with fast internet, Stadia felt slightly off to experienced players. Not broken. Just wrong in a way you couldn't ignore.

Problem two: the game library was thin at launch and stayed thin. The reason? Developers refused to invest in Stadia-exclusive titles. And the reason for that was Google's history — a well-documented pattern of killing products with no warning. Google Reader. Google Buzz. Google Allo. Google Glass. There's an entire website called 'Killed by Google' that was tracking over 270 discontinued products. No sane developer studio was going to bet their budget on a platform run by a company with that track record."

**[THE PIVOT — 2:30–3:15]**
"In early 2021, Google made a startling admission without quite admitting it: they announced they were shutting down Stadia's internal game development studios and ending plans for exclusive titles. The head of Stadia gaming, Jade Raymond — who Google had recruited with considerable fanfare — departed. Google rebranded Stadia as a 'platform technology' business that it would license to other companies.

This was not a strategic pivot. This was a flag planted in the ground that said: the consumer product has failed. Google just kept the storefront open for another 18 months while they figured out what to do, which mostly confused and frustrated the users who were still buying games on it."

**[THE SHUTDOWN — 3:15–4:15]**
"On September 29, 2022, Google announced Stadia was closing on January 18, 2023. The shutdown was, in fairness, handled better than most Google product closures: full refunds for all hardware and game purchases were issued. Most users got their money back. But the refund didn't return game saves, archived communities, or the time spent building a library on a platform that no longer exists.

And here's the thing about Google's refund: it was generous precisely because Google could afford to be generous. Smaller companies that made exclusive Stadia games, that hired teams for Stadia development, that built businesses around the platform — they got nothing. The generosity to consumers came at the expense of the partner ecosystem."

**[THE GRAVEYARD CONTEXT — 4:15–4:50]**
"At the time of Stadia's death, the 'Killed by Google' website listed 270 discontinued products. 270. If you're a developer deciding whether to build for a platform, and the platform owner has a 270-product graveyard visible to anyone who Googles it — you need a very good reason to say yes. Stadia never gave developers that reason, which is why the content library never gave consumers a reason, which is why the consumers never came, which is why the developers were right."

**[LESSONS — 4:50–5:35]**
"Stadia failed because it asked for trust it hadn't earned. Trust from developers — to build for a platform run by a company that abandons products. Trust from consumers — to buy games on a platform that might not exist next year. Trust from the broader ecosystem — to build businesses around Google's commitment to gaming. The product wasn't terrible. The reputation was. And in platform businesses, reputation is architecture."

**[OUTRO — 5:35–6:00]**
"A $200 million platform. A game library that simply stopped existing. And a graveyard of 270 products that told developers everything they needed to know from the start. Subscribe — we cover the decisions that were obviously going to fail and the people who made them anyway."
