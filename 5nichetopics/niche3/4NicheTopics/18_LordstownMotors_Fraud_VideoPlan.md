## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,050 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In 2019, Donald Trump visited a shuttered GM factory in Lordstown, Ohio and told auto workers their jobs were coming back — because of an electric truck startup called Lordstown Motors. Workers cheered. Investors poured in millions. The company went public via SPAC at $1.6 billion. And then a short-seller research firm discovered that the 100,000-plus pre-orders the company had been publicizing to investors were almost entirely fake. The SEC charged the founders with fraud. The community was left behind again. This is Lordstown — and it's both a startup story and a story about what happens when political hype meets Wall Street greed in a small Ohio town."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Lordstown Motors was founded in 2018 by Steve Burns, who had previously led Workhorse Group, another EV startup. Burns purchased the shuttered General Motors Lordstown assembly plant — closed in 2019 at an enormous cost to the local economy — for just $20 million. The plan: produce the Endurance, an electric pickup truck for commercial fleets, using a unique hub motor design with four independent in-wheel motors instead of a traditional drivetrain.

The location was powerful PR. Lordstown, Ohio was Rust Belt heartland. The closure of the GM plant had devastated the region — 1,400 direct jobs lost, thousands more downstream. A new electric vehicle company buying that factory and promising to bring it back to life was a narrative that politicians, investors, and media could not resist. And they didn't."

**[THE PRE-ORDER FRAUD — 1:30–2:30]**
"In March 2021, short-seller firm Hindenburg Research published a detailed report titled 'Lordstown Motors: An EV Startup Lacking in Customers, Cash, and a Clear Path to Production.' The core allegation: Lordstown had been publicly citing 100,000-plus non-binding pre-orders as evidence of commercial demand — but Hindenburg's investigation found that many of those orders came from small fleet companies with no financial capacity to purchase the trucks, individuals who had signed up for free with zero commitment, and in some cases, people who appeared to have no relationship with fleet purchasing at all.

The company had been presenting these soft expressions of interest to investors as evidence of a robust sales pipeline. That gap between what the orders were and what they were represented as being — is, legally, fraud."

**[EXECUTIVE DEPARTURES — 2:30–3:15]**
"The board launched an internal investigation. It confirmed enough of Hindenburg's findings to force a reckoning. In June 2021, CEO Steve Burns and CFO Julio Rodriguez both resigned. Lordstown simultaneously disclosed that it didn't have enough cash to begin production and might have to consider bankruptcy. The company had been preparing to manufacture a vehicle while its two senior executives apparently had not fully disclosed the state of the order book to their own board.

The SEC launched a formal investigation. The DOJ issued subpoenas. The company that was supposed to bring hope to Lordstown was now the subject of multiple federal inquiries."

**[FOXCONN — THE UNLIKELY SAVIOR — 3:15–4:00]**
"In a twist no one saw coming, Foxconn — the Taiwanese manufacturing giant that builds Apple's iPhones — agreed to purchase the Lordstown factory for $230 million and signed a manufacturing partnership. It was a genuine lifeline. With Foxconn's manufacturing expertise and capital, the Endurance went into limited production — a few hundred vehicles were built and delivered to fleet customers. For a moment, it looked like Lordstown might survive in some form.

Then Lordstown sued Foxconn, claiming the manufacturer had reneged on additional investment commitments. Foxconn counter-sued. The relationship collapsed. In 2023, Lordstown filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. The factory that GM closed, that Lordstown Motors bought, that Foxconn purchased — remained standing. The businesses around it did not."

**[SEC FRAUD CHARGES — 4:00–4:30]**
"In June 2023, the SEC charged Steve Burns and former CFO Julio Rodriguez with securities fraud. The allegations were specific: Burns knew the pre-orders were not genuine purchase commitments but continued to represent them publicly as evidence of strong commercial demand. The SEC alleged this misled investors who funded the company's SPAC listing based on those representations."

**[LESSONS — 4:30–5:15]**
"Lordstown encapsulates several failures at once. The SPAC structure let it access public capital without the scrutiny of a traditional IPO. The political narrative — saving Rust Belt jobs, America's EV future — made investors want to believe, which made them skip the questions they should have asked. Non-binding pre-orders are not revenue. A famous factory is not a business plan. And the community that needed real economic relief paid the price for a story that was partly fiction."

**[OUTRO — 5:15–5:30]**
"100,000 pre-orders. $1.6 billion SPAC valuation. A presidential visit. SEC fraud charges. And a town that needed jobs — still waiting. Subscribe — we cover the stories that remind us why due diligence matters."
