## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,000 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In April 2020, two of the most powerful people in Hollywood launched a billion-dollar streaming app. They had $1.75 billion, every A-list celebrity in the business, and — by extraordinary coincidence — launched it during the greatest home entertainment boom in human history: a global pandemic that locked everyone inside with their phones. And still. STILL. They failed. Quibi lasted six months. This is the most expensive lesson in product-market fit ever paid."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Quibi — short for 'Quick Bites' — was the brainchild of Jeffrey Katzenberg, the Hollywood legend behind DreamWorks Animation and a career that spanned decades of blockbusters. His co-pilot was Meg Whitman, former CEO of eBay and Hewlett-Packard. Between them: decades of experience, massive networks, and genuine credibility in both entertainment and tech.

Their concept: premium video content delivered in episodes under 10 minutes, designed specifically for mobile phones, in portrait orientation. Like TV, but for your commute. Like movies, but for your lunch break. Before a single episode aired, they had raised $1.75 billion. Every major Hollywood studio backed it. Katzenberg secured content deals with Spielberg, Guillermo del Toro, and virtually every A-lister who returned a call. The launch event felt like a cultural moment. What happened next felt like a comedy."

**[THE LAUNCH — 1:30–2:30]**
"Quibi launched on April 6, 2020. Two weeks into a global pandemic that had shut down every city on earth. The app built for people on the go — commuting, waiting in airport lounges, grabbing lunch between meetings — launched into a world where nobody was going anywhere. People were at home. On couches. In front of 60-inch televisions. With Netflix, Disney+, HBO Max, and YouTube all competing furiously for their attention.

And here's the part that made tech Twitter lose its mind: at launch, you could not cast Quibi to a TV. At all. Phones only. A company with $1.75 billion somehow hadn't built a feature that Samsung and Apple shipped for free years earlier. Users complained immediately. Katzenberg's response: he blamed the pandemic, calling the timing 'no one's fault.' The internet had opinions about that."

**[THE PRODUCT PROBLEMS — 2:30–3:30]**
"Even setting aside the timing catastrophe, Quibi had deeper problems that no pandemic could explain. There was no social sharing. You couldn't screenshot anything from the app. In an era where TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts were growing entirely through viral sharing — where discoverability was social — Quibi had deliberately built an anti-viral, anti-social product. No clips. No sharing. No way for a great piece of content to spread.

The content itself — despite costing an average of $100,000 per minute to produce — failed to generate a single cultural moment. In six months, did you hear anyone say 'have you seen that Quibi show?' There was no 'Tiger King' moment. No breakout series. The most talked-about Quibi moment was a news segment about an earthquake — which briefly became a meme. That was it. The entire cultural output of $1.75 billion."

**[THE COLLAPSE — 3:30–4:30]**
"By August 2020 — just four months in — reports surfaced that Quibi was exploring a sale. Katzenberg reportedly called Disney, NBCUniversal, Apple, and Facebook personally. All passed. Not politely maybe, but they passed. On October 21, 2020, Quibi announced it was shutting down in two weeks. The speed of the announcement mirrored the speed of everything at Quibi — they moved fast, just not in the right direction.

The content library — hundreds of hours of celebrity programming — was sold off in pieces. Most Quibi originals simply ceased to exist. They weren't picked up by other platforms, weren't released online, weren't archived meaningfully. They just disappeared. Along with $1.75 billion and any remaining credibility for the concept of phone-native premium video."

**[LESSONS — 4:30–5:30]**
"Quibi's failure comes down to one thing: they built a product for a behavior that consumers could already satisfy for free, better, on platforms they were already using. YouTube existed. TikTok existed. Instagram existed. The question 'why would someone pay for Quibi when they have this?' was never satisfactorily answered — because it couldn't be.

Additionally: launching a 'go-somewhere' product during a stay-at-home order is the kind of timing mistake that no amount of intelligence or money overcomes. Sometimes the universe is just very literal about product-market fit."

**[OUTRO — 5:30–5:50]**
"$1.75 billion. The greatest home streaming audience in history literally locked indoors. Zero cultural footprint. If you cannot name a single Quibi show right now — that's the entire post-mortem. Subscribe for more."
