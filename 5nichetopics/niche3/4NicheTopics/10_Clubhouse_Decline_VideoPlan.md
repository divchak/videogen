## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,000 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In early 2021, someone sold a Clubhouse invitation on eBay for $100. An invitation. To a free app. Elon Musk held a room. Oprah dropped in unannounced. The app was valued at $4 billion after being around for less than a year. And then, with a speed that was almost artistic, it became a ghost town. The rooms are still technically there. The speakers are just — gone. What happened to the app the whole world needed to be on — right up until they didn't?"

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Clubhouse launched in March 2020, created by Paul Davison and Rohan Seth at a small company called Alpha Exploration. The concept was beautifully simple: audio-only chat rooms, open to the public, where anyone could listen and selected speakers could take the stage. No video. No text. Just voices. In a world suddenly isolated by lockdowns and starved of spontaneous social interaction, the timing was perfect. The invite-only launch created artificial scarcity and massive FOMO. Having a Clubhouse invite in mid-2020 was like having the right backstage pass.

By January 2021, Andreessen Horowitz valued Clubhouse at $1 billion — a unicorn after less than 10 months. In February 2021, it hit 10 million weekly active users. Elon Musk appeared in a room and the app literally crashed from demand. Mark Zuckerberg held rooms. There were rooms about crypto, rooms about startups, rooms about nothing at all. Everyone was on Clubhouse — and everyone wanted you to know they were on Clubhouse."

**[THE PEAK — 1:30–2:15]**
"The peak was February 2021. Clubhouse was everywhere. Tech Twitter talked about it constantly. Non-tech people were confused but intrigued. Brands wanted in. Celebrities wanted in. Journalists wanted in — to write about who was in. The network effects felt self-reinforcing and unstoppable. The $4 billion valuation followed shortly after. Conventional wisdom said Clubhouse was going to become the next great social platform — the audio layer of the internet. And then the competitors arrived."

**[THE CRACKS — 2:15–3:15]**
"In May 2021, Twitter launched Spaces — a near-identical audio room feature — directly inside the Twitter app that 400 million people already used. Facebook launched Live Audio Rooms. LinkedIn launched its own version. Spotify launched Greenroom. Reddit launched Talk. In about four months, every major platform had shipped a Clubhouse clone — for free, to users who were already there, on apps they already opened every day.

And Clubhouse had a structural weakness that only became visible now: the content was live and ephemeral. Nothing was recorded. You had to be there at the exact right moment. As novelty faded and alternatives multiplied, the effort required to discover and attend a good room stopped feeling worthwhile. The platform had not built recording, content discovery, or creator monetization — three things that keep people coming back when the initial hype subsides."

**[THE FALL — 3:15–4:15]**
"The numbers tell the story without sentiment. In February 2021, Clubhouse had 9.6 million monthly downloads. By May 2021 — three months later — downloads had fallen to under 1 million. The app had lost 90% of its new user momentum in one quarter. By the end of 2021, Clubhouse dropped the invite-only model and opened to Android — the two moves that were supposed to unlock mass adoption. Instead, they stripped away the exclusivity that had made the platform feel special, without replacing it with anything that made it feel necessary.

In April 2023, Clubhouse laid off over half its staff. The founders published a blog about 'resetting the product' — developer-speak for 'the original model didn't work and we're not sure what comes next.' The app remains live. The rooms still exist. But the 10 million people who were there in February 2021 have mostly moved on."

**[LESSONS — 4:15–5:15]**
"Clubhouse is one of the cleanest lessons in the difference between a feature and a platform. A feature is something any company with enough engineering resources can ship. Twitter shipped it in months. A platform is something with deep network effects, switching costs, and a reason to exist independently. Clubhouse was a feature — an excellent, well-timed, genuinely innovative feature — but one that sat on its own island.

When the feature was embedded in platforms people already lived on, users didn't need to travel to the island anymore. The moat wasn't the technology. It was the community. And community, unlike code, cannot be transferred by moving fast."

**[OUTRO — 5:15–5:30]**
"10 million users in February 2021. A $4 billion valuation. And by May 2021, 90% of that momentum — gone. The rooms still exist. Nobody's home. Subscribe for more."
