## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~850 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"On April 26, 2021, Basecamp's co-founders sent their employees an internal memo. It banned political discussions at work, eliminated employee committees, and cut benefits. Within days, one-third of the company's employees had quit publicly and en masse. This is what happens when a software company decides to become a case study in how not to manage people."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Basecamp is a project management SaaS company founded in 1999 by Jason Fried and David Heinemeier Hansson — known as DHH. For two decades, they were celebrated as the gold standard of opinionated software culture. Their books — 'Getting Real,' 'Rework,' and 'Remote' — sold millions of copies and influenced an entire generation of startup founders.

They preached radical transparency, healthy work-life balance, and strong company values. They built a product used by millions. They were respected, admired role models. And then came the memo."

**[THE MEMO — 1:30–2:30]**
"The April 2021 memo, published publicly by DHH and Fried, announced five significant policy changes: no more political discussions on company platforms, elimination of 'societal obligations' beyond work, disbanding of diversity and inclusion committees, ending 360-degree feedback and peer reviews, and cutting a range of employee benefits including fitness stipends and education allowances.

The memo cited a need to keep the workplace focused on work — arguing that political discussions were 'not healthy' for the team. Context later emerged: there had been internal conflict when employees raised concerns about a list of 'funny customer names' that had been maintained internally for years — a list that included names that employees of color found racially insensitive."

**[THE WALKOUT — 2:30–3:15]**
"The employee reaction was immediate and public. Basecamp had a small team of around 57 people. Within days, approximately 20 employees — roughly one-third of the company — announced their resignations publicly. Many posted detailed farewell notes explaining their reasons.

The Twitter and tech media coverage was extensive. DHH and Fried responded publicly, doubling down on their position. The discourse became a proxy war for a much larger conversation about political speech in workplaces — with Basecamp at the center."

**[THE PRODUCT FALLOUT — 3:15–4:00]**
"Basecamp at the time was about to launch a major new product: Hey — a paid email service that competed with Gmail and Apple Mail. The timing could not have been worse. Hey launched amid the public controversy, and the reputational damage affected its adoption.

Basecamp's user community was shaken. Customers who had chosen Basecamp partly because of its stated values publicly discussed switching tools. The incident surfaced a deeper question: when companies build their brand around culture and values, what happens when those values conflict internally?"

**[AFTERMATH — 4:00–4:45]**
"DHH and Fried continued publishing blogs defending their decisions. Basecamp and Hey remained operational products. However, the cultural authority they had spent two decades building was permanently damaged. The company that wrote the books on healthy startup culture had become the story of unhealthy leadership.

By 2022, DHH had become increasingly outspoken on political platforms including Twitter/X — the exact behavior the memo claimed to want to remove from the workplace."

**[LESSONS — 4:45–5:30]**
"Basecamp teaches a critical lesson about credibility and consistency. Companies that build their brand on values and culture are held to a higher standard. When those values are perceived as selectively applied — banning employee political speech while the founders are politically outspoken — the contradiction destroys trust.

You can set workplace policies. But if they look like protecting leadership from accountability, employees will call you on it. Especially publicly. Especially in tech."

**[OUTRO — 5:30–5:50]**
"One memo. One-third of employees gone. And a 20-year reputation for great culture — gone with them. Subscribe for more."
