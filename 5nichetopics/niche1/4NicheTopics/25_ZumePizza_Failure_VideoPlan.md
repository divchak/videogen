## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~800 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"What if you put robots in a pizza truck, used algorithms to bake the pizza on the way to delivery, and called it the future of food? Then when that didn't work, pivoted to sustainable packaging. Then to automated food trucks. Then to drone delivery. Then to something else. Zume Pizza raised $375 million from SoftBank, pivoted five times, and still failed. This is the most confused startup in food tech history."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Zume Pizza was founded in 2015 by Alex Garden and Julia Collins in Mountain View, California. The original innovation was genuinely interesting: robot arms in customized trucks would pre-assemble pizzas in a central kitchen, and then a second set of robots in the delivery trucks would bake the pizzas en route — so the pizza arrived hot out of the oven, precisely at the moment of delivery.

It was solving a real problem: pizza quality degrades during delivery. The engineering was impressive. The concept attracted attention — and then SoftBank noticed."

**[SOFTBANK MAKES IT WORSE — 1:30–2:15]**
"In November 2018, SoftBank invested $375 million in Zume — at a valuation of around $2.25 billion. This is Masayoshi Son's style: go big immediately. The money was intended to scale Zume beyond pizza — to build a complete 'food-as-a-service' platform, with automated kitchens and trucks that could make any food on demand.

The $375 million was far more money than Zume's actual business needed or could absorb responsibly. The company hired aggressively — growing to over 1,400 employees — and began pursuing ambitions far beyond its original pizza concept."

**[THE PIVOT PARADE — 2:15–3:15]**
"With SoftBank's backing, Zume launched a dizzying series of strategic pivots:
- Pizza delivery via robot trucks → Struggled with economics and regulatory complexity
- Sustainable packaging business → Pivoted away from food entirely
- Food platform/marketplace → Attempted to license the technology to restaurant brands
- Drone delivery experiment → Never scaled
- Smart kitchen appliances for consumer homes → Launched then dropped

Each pivot required new R&D, new team structures, and burned through the $375 million faster than any pizza could be baked."

**[THE COLLAPSE — 3:15–4:00]**
"In January 2020, Zume laid off approximately 360 employees — about half its workforce — and shut down its pizza delivery business entirely. The very thing the company was founded to do. It pivoted to sustainable packaging full-time. In 2023, the packaging business also collapsed. Zume shut down completely.

SoftBank wrote off its $375 million investment. The robot pizza trucks were quietly auctioned off."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"Zume's story is a perfect case study in what happens when too much capital is given too soon to a company before it's proven its core business. The $375 million SoftBank investment forced Zume to think at a scale it wasn't ready for. Instead of perfecting robot pizza delivery — which had merit — it tried to become everything, and became nothing.

Too much money, too fast, from the wrong investors, can be just as deadly as too little."

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"$375 million. Robot pizza. Five pivots. Zero successful businesses. Subscribe for more startup lessons delivered hot."
