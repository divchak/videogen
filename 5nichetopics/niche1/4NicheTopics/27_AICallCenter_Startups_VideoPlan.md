## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~880 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Between 2018 and 2023, hundreds of startups raised billions of dollars promising to replace or radically transform customer service with artificial intelligence. 'Goodbye hold music. Hello AI.' The sales decks were extraordinary. The results were mostly dismal. This is the story of the AI call center wave that crashed — and what it reveals about selling AI to enterprises."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Customer service is one of the largest operational costs in business. US companies spend over $300 billion annually on customer support. Every company in the world has a contact center. The workforce is enormous, the work is repetitive, and the customer experience is universally frustrating. It was the perfect target for AI disruption.

Dozens of well-funded startups emerged: Cogito Analytics (using AI to coach call center agents), Observe.AI (conversation intelligence), Cresta AI (real-time coaching), Directly (deflecting calls with AI), Replicant (fully automated voice AI), and dozens more. Venture capital poured in."

**[THE ENTERPRISE AI REALITY — 1:30–2:30]**
"But selling AI to enterprises is fundamentally different from consumer apps. Enterprise sales cycles are long — 12 to 18 months. Procurement is complex. IT security reviews are exhaustive. Integration with legacy systems takes months. And the promised AI performance rarely survived contact with real-world customer data.

Most AI call center solutions worked impressively in controlled demos. They struggled significantly in live environments where customers were upset, used regional accents, switched between topics unexpectedly, or used non-standard vocabulary. The gap between demo performance and live performance was the story of an entire industry."

**[COGITO — 2:30–3:15]**
"Cogito Analytics, based in Boston, was one of the more serious players. It used AI to analyze call center agent behavior in real time — measuring empathy signals, speaking pace, and call structure — and coached agents with real-time nudges. It raised over $125 million. The technology had genuine scientific basis from MIT research.

But the business struggled to scale. Enterprises signed pilots but many didn't convert to enterprise-wide deployments. By 2023, Cogito was significantly restructured — reducing headcount and narrowing its focus. A company with genuine technology but a challenging path to profitability."

**[THE GPT DISRUPTION — 3:15–4:00]**
"Then came GPT-4 and the large language model revolution in 2023. Suddenly the most capable AI customer service technology was available from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google — at API costs per interaction that were fractions of what specialized startups charged. Major platforms like Salesforce, Zendesk, and Intercom integrated LLMs directly into their customer service tools.

The specialized AI call center startups were now competing against the platforms their customers already used, powered by base models more capable than their own. Many pivoted, scaled down, or exited quietly."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"The AI call center wave reveals a pattern that has repeated across enterprise AI: the technology works better in demos than in production, enterprise sales is slower and more expensive than consumer sales, and platform incumbents will eventually integrate AI capabilities that neutralize specialized startups.

The lesson for both startups and investors: enterprise AI adoption curves are long. A company that raises on promises of rapid displacement of human workers is making a bet that rarely pays off as quickly as projected."

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"Billions raised. The $300 billion customer service market still mostly uses humans. Subscribe for more AI reality checks."
