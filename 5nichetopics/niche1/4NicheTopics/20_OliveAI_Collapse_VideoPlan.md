## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~820 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Olive AI was once the most valuable health tech startup in America. It promised to automate the most tedious and costly back-office tasks in hospitals — prior authorizations, claims processing, insurance verification — using artificial intelligence. It was valued at $4 billion. Then, quietly and almost without warning, it dismantled itself piece by piece. This is the Olive AI story."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Olive AI was founded in 2012 by Sean Lane in Columbus, Ohio. The pitch was simple but powerful: hospitals spend enormous amounts of time and money on administrative tasks — processing insurance claims, handling prior authorizations, verifying patient eligibility. These tasks are repetitive, rule-based, and perfect candidates for automation.

Olive's technology used robotic process automation — essentially software bots that mimicked human computer interactions — to perform these tasks automatically. The addressable market was real: US hospitals spend over $1 trillion on administrative costs annually."

**[THE GROWTH AND THE HYPE — 1:30–2:30]**
"Olive raised money aggressively. By 2021, the company had raised over $900 million across multiple funding rounds — including a massive $400 million round that pushed its valuation to $4 billion. It was named one of the most innovative companies in healthcare. It claimed to serve over 900 hospitals and health systems.

CEO Sean Lane was an engaging speaker who positioned Olive at the forefront of healthcare AI. The company hired aggressively, grew to over 900 employees, and set ambitious targets for automating processes that would save hospitals billions."

**[THE PERFORMANCE QUESTIONS — 2:30–3:15]**
"But questions about Olive's actual performance began emerging. Healthcare AI automation is significantly harder than it appears in demos. Hospital IT environments are fragmented, legacy-heavy, and resistant to change. Integration challenges were significant.

Internal reports suggested that Olive's automation was less reliable than marketed — requiring significant human oversight to function correctly, which undermined the cost savings it promised. The ROI claims that had won contracts began to face scrutiny from hospital CFOs who weren't seeing the promised savings."

**[THE SELF-DISMANTLING — 3:15–4:00]**
"Starting in 2022, Olive began selling off its business units. It sold its pharmacy automation unit. It sold its clinical documentation unit. It laid off large portions of its workforce — cutting from over 900 to a small fraction. Each divestiture was framed as a 'strategic focus' — but the pattern was clear: the company was unwinding.

By late 2023, Olive AI had sold or shut down most of its operations. The remaining entity was a shell of its former self. The $4 billion valuation was a memory."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"Olive AI illustrates the specific challenges of healthcare technology. Healthcare operates under strict compliance requirements, has fragmented IT infrastructure, and involves life-or-death stakes that make rapid automation extremely difficult. The gap between what AI automation demos look like and what it delivers in live hospital environments was enormous.

Investors were dazzled by the massive addressable market without asking whether the technology was actually working in practice. When the technology underdelivers, no amount of valuation can hide the problem forever."

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"$4 billion. 900 hospitals. And an AI that quietly stopped delivering. Subscribe for more of what healthcare AI actually costs."
