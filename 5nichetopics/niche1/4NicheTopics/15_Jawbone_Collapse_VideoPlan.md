## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~820 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In 2016, Jawbone was valued at $1.5 billion. The year before that, it was $3.2 billion. By 2017, it had ceased operations — without a press conference, without a bankruptcy announcement, without a farewell post. It just quietly stopped. $930 million raised. Zero returned to investors. The death was as silent as the company's final Bluetooth speaker."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Jawbone was founded in 1999 by Hosain Rahman and Alexander Asseily. For its first decade, it was primarily a Bluetooth headset company — those earpieces people wore in the early 2000s. By the mid-2000s, it had moved into Bluetooth speakers — the iconic Jambox line became a cultural staple.

Then came the health tech pivot. In 2011, Jawbone launched the UP fitness tracker — a slim wristband that tracked steps, sleep, and activity. This was before Fitbit had gone public, before Apple Watch existed. Jawbone was in the right market at the right time."

**[THE FITBIT WAR — 1:30–2:30]**
"But Jawbone and Fitbit were locked in direct competition. Fitbit went public in 2015, raising $732 million. The public market gave Fitbit capital to invest in product development, retail distribution, and branding at scale. Jawbone remained private, dependent on funding rounds.

Jawbone's products suffered from quality problems. The UP band had a 2011 recall due to skin irritation issues affecting users. Subsequent models had connectivity problems. Returns were high. User reviews were mixed. While Fitbit was winning at retail, Jawbone was struggling with execution."

**[THE FUNDING TRAP — 2:30–3:15]**
"Rather than scaling back, Jawbone raised more money — much more. It raised $300 million in 2014, $165 million in 2015, and $165 million again in 2016. Each round came at a lower valuation — a down round — a signal that investors were losing confidence.

The company spent lavishly on product lines — speakers, headsets, fitness bands — without dominating any of them. Reports from former employees described internal chaos, high executive turnover, and a founder who resisted strategic pivots even as the business deteriorated."

**[THE LAWSUIT WITH FITBIT — 3:15–3:50]**
"In a dramatic subplot, Jawbone sued Fitbit in 2015 for allegedly stealing trade secrets after several Jawbone engineers left to join Fitbit. The lawsuit dragged on for years and cost both companies significant resources. The courts ultimately ruled largely in Fitbit's favor. The legal battle drained Jawbone's resources and management attention at a critical time."

**[THE QUIET DEATH — 3:50–4:30]**
"In mid-2017, Jawbone simply stopped. There was no public announcement. The company stopped responding to customer support requests. Its products went dark. The assets were quietly liquidated. Hosain Rahman transitioned to starting a new healthcare data company called Jawbone Health Hub.

Investors who had put in nearly $1 billion got essentially nothing. Employees lost their jobs with minimal notice. It was one of Silicon Valley's most silent, undignified endings."

**[LESSONS — 4:30–5:15]**
"Jawbone is a story about the danger of funding as a strategy. Raising more money doesn't fix product problems, execution failures, or competitive disadvantage. Jawbone funded its way through every problem rather than solving the underlying issues. Eventually, investors stopped funding — and there was nothing left.

The difference between Jawbone and Fitbit ultimately wasn't innovation. It was execution, focus, and access to public capital."

**[OUTRO — 5:15–5:30]**
"$3.2 billion valuation. $930 million raised. No bankruptcy filing. No farewell. Just silence. Subscribe for more quiet collapses."
