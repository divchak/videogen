## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~880 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In 2018, Bird became the fastest startup in history to reach a $1 billion valuation — doing it in just 400 days. Electric scooters were going to transform cities. Investors poured in billions. Five years later, Bird filed for bankruptcy and its stock was delisted after trading for just a fraction of a cent. So what went wrong with the scooter revolution?"

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Bird was founded in 2017 by Travis VanderZanden, a former Lyft and Uber executive. The premise was dockless electric scooters: drop a fleet of scooters in a city, let users unlock them with a smartphone app, charge a flat fee plus per-minute rates. No parking infrastructure needed. No fixed docking stations. Just scooters everywhere.

Cities were initially receptive — or at least overwhelmed. Bird deployed scooters in Santa Monica before getting permits, asking forgiveness rather than permission. The startup playbook: move fast, create facts on the ground, then negotiate. It worked enough to raise $400 million from Sequoia, Accel, and others."

**[THE ECONOMICS PROBLEM — 1:30–2:45]**
"The fundamental problem with scooters is the unit economics. Each scooter costs $500 to manufacture. It needs to be charged every night — requiring someone to collect it, charge it, and redeploy it. It gets damaged, stolen, and vandalized. The average lifespan of a Bird scooter in 2018 was estimated at just 28 days.

Do the math: a $500 scooter lasting 28 days, earning roughly $3-5 per ride with a few rides per day — it barely breaks even before it's destroyed or lost. Bird needed scale and longer scooter lifespans to make the economics work. The scooters of 2018 simply weren't durable enough."

**[THE CITY BACKLASH — 2:45–3:30]**
"Cities began pushing back. Scooters cluttered sidewalks. Riders ignored safety rules. Pedestrians complained. San Francisco initially banned scooters entirely before creating a strict permitting system that cut the number of operators. Paris, which had embraced scooters enthusiastically, voted to ban them entirely in 2023.

The regulatory environment became increasingly hostile, driving up operational costs and limiting the cities Bird could serve."

**[COVID AND THE SPAC TRAP — 3:30–4:15]**
"COVID-19 in 2020 devastated Bird's business — urban mobility collapsed. Bird survived through the pandemic but chose a SPAC merger to go public in 2021 — valuing the company at $2.3 billion. Going public via SPAC bypassed the rigorous due diligence of a traditional IPO.

The shares immediately began falling. By 2022, Bird was warned by the NYSE that it faced delisting. The stock dropped to under $0.01 — a penny stock. In November 2023, Bird filed for bankruptcy. The assets were acquired at a fraction of their previous value."

**[LESSONS — 4:15–5:00]**
"Bird's story illustrates the danger of scaling a business before the unit economics are proven. When each scooter is losing money, owning more scooters loses more money. No amount of venture capital fixes a broken cost structure. The SPAC route allowed Bird to sidestep hard questions about profitability — questions that became unavoidable once public scrutiny arrived."

**[OUTRO — 5:00–5:15]**
"Fastest startup to $1 billion. Bankrupt in 5 years. The scooter revolution was real — the business model wasn't ready. Subscribe for more."
