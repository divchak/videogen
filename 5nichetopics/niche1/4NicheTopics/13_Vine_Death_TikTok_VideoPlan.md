## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~900 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In 2016, Twitter shut down Vine — the 6-second video app that invented the format that TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts are now worth hundreds of billions of dollars on. Twitter had bought Vine for $30 million before it even launched. And then they let it die. This is one of the most expensive strategic mistakes in social media history."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Vine launched in January 2013, just weeks after Twitter acquired it for an estimated $30 million. The concept was simple: six seconds of looping video. That constraint — the six-second limit — turned out to be genius. It forced creativity. Comedians found it. Musicians found it. Teenagers found it.

By 2015, Vine had over 200 million users. Stars like Logan Paul, Lele Pons, and Shawn Mendes — who would later become massive celebrities — built their first audiences entirely on Vine. Brands experimented with it. Pop culture loved it."

**[TWITTER'S NEGLECT — 1:30–2:30]**
"But Twitter largely left Vine to run on its own. There was little investment in the product, no monetization for creators, and no mechanism to pay the people who were creating the platform's value. Meanwhile, creators on YouTube were earning significant money through ad revenue. Instagram introduced video. Snapchat launched Stories.

Vine's creators began openly complaining that they couldn't make a living on the platform. In 2016, a group of Vine's top creators — who collectively had hundreds of millions of followers — approached Vine management with an ultimatum: pay us $1.2 million each or we're leaving. Vine said no."

**[THE DEATH — 2:30–3:15]**
"On October 27, 2016, Twitter announced it was shutting down Vine. The company was already under financial pressure — Twitter itself was struggling and had just rejected acquisition offers from Google, Disney, and Salesforce.

The Vine shutdown was swift and brutal. The app went from live to dark in a matter of months. Users scrambled to download their videos. A vast archive of internet culture — billions of 6-second moments — was nearly lost. A partial archive was saved by archivists at the Internet Archive."

**[THE IRONY — 3:15–4:15]**
"The devastating irony came just two years later. In 2018, TikTok — a Chinese-owned short video app — launched in Western markets using almost exactly Vine's format. TikTok grew explosively, reaching 1 billion monthly active users. Instagram launched Reels directly in response. YouTube launched Shorts. The entire short-form video economy — now worth hundreds of billions — is built on the format Vine pioneered.

Twitter's leadership would later publicly admit regret about shutting down Vine. Jack Dorsey said it was 'one of the more regrettable things' he'd done at the company."

**[THE DOM HOFMANN ATTEMPT — 4:15–4:45]**
"Vine's co-founder Dom Hofmann tried twice to revive the format. V2 was announced in 2017 and eventually launched as Byte in 2020. Byte received positive early reviews but struggled to achieve critical mass. It was later rebranded as Clash and then shut down — never capturing the lightning in a bottle that Vine had originally created."

**[LESSONS — 4:45–5:30]**
"Vine's story is about the cost of ignoring creators. Platforms are only as good as the content on them. If you refuse to monetize the people creating value on your platform, they will leave. And if you don't invest in keeping your competitive advantage — someone else will take it.

Twitter had the short-form video format years before TikTok. They chose not to invest in it. That strategic failure is now worth billions."

**[OUTRO — 5:30–5:50]**
"$30 million acquisition. 200 million users. Shut down in 2016. Worth hundreds of billions today — just not to Twitter. Subscribe for more."
