## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~880 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"IRL — which stood for 'In Real Life' — was a social networking app that claimed to have 20 million monthly active users. Investors including SoftBank valued it at $1.17 billion — making it a unicorn. When the company shut down in 2023, an SEC investigation had found that 95% of those 20 million users were bots, fake accounts, or automated activity. The entire user base was almost entirely fabricated."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"IRL was founded in 2017 by Abraham Shafi, who positioned it as a social discovery app for real-world events and activities — a way to find out what your friends were actually doing and join them in person. The timing seemed perfect: there was growing cultural concern about social media driving people apart, and IRL's message was about bringing people together.

The app evolved to include group messaging, event creation, and community features. It targeted younger users. It raised $16 million in 2019 and then, during the pandemic, secured a massive $170 million funding round in 2021 — valuing it at $1.17 billion."

**[THE BOT PROBLEM — 1:30–2:30]**
"The SEC investigation, which came after the company's 2023 closure, revealed a stunning finding: approximately 95% of IRL's reported user activity was generated by bots or automated systems, not real human users. The company had been reporting 20 million monthly active users to investors — but the genuine human users numbered closer to 1 million.

This is one of the most extreme cases of metric inflation in startup history. The company's entire valuation thesis — that it was a rapidly growing social network with a massive user base — was built almost entirely on fabricated data."

**[THE INVESTOR STORY — 2:30–3:15]**
"SoftBank's Vision Fund led the $170 million round. Andreessen Horowitz was also an investor. These are some of the world's most sophisticated technology investors. How did they miss a 95% bot problem?

The investigation suggests that IRL used sophisticated techniques to inflate metrics — creating bot accounts that appeared to behave like real users, with activity patterns that could pass casual inspection. The due diligence process, which relies partly on trusting the company's own reported data, was insufficient to detect the fraud."

**[THE SHUTDOWN AND INVESTIGATION — 3:15–4:15]**
"In June 2023, IRL abruptly shut down and laid off its staff. The company cited 'regulatory issues' as the reason. The SEC launched an investigation into potential securities fraud. Abraham Shafi departed.

The SEC's investigation concluded that investors had been materially misled about the user base. A significant portion of the raised capital — meant to grow a genuine social network — had been used to sustain a business built on manufactured metrics."

**[THE DEEPER PROBLEM — 4:15–5:00]**
"IRL highlights a systemic problem in social media investing: user count is the primary metric investors use to value social apps, but user count is the easiest metric to fake. Unlike revenue (which shows up in bank accounts), or physical delivery (which requires actual logistics), digital user activity can be manufactured by sophisticated bot networks.

The irony: IRL — 'In Real Life' — was perhaps the least 'real' startup in recent memory."

**[LESSONS — 5:00–5:45]**
"For investors: social app user metrics require independent verification, not just company-provided data. For observers: the pressure to show growth in Silicon Valley creates incentives to inflate metrics. And for consumers: the apps that claim massive user numbers should be viewed skeptically — engagement is the real signal, and it's harder to fake.

The IRL story is a cautionary tale for an era when user metrics have replaced revenue as the primary signal of startup value."

**[OUTRO — 5:45–6:00]**
"$170 million raised. 20 million claimed users. 95% bots. The most ironic startup name in history. Subscribe for more."
