## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~920 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In January 2021, Peloton's stock hit $167 per share. The pandemic had made the connected exercise bike a symbol of a new way to live, work, and work out. A year and a half later, the stock had fallen to under $9. There had been a treadmill recall after a child's death. The CEO had resigned. And Peloton was desperately trying to pivot from a hardware company to a software company. Here's the full story."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Peloton was founded in 2012 by John Foley. The concept was premium connected fitness: a stationary bike with a large touchscreen that streamed live and on-demand cycling classes with celebrity instructors. The experience was genuinely excellent — it replicated the energy of a boutique fitness class from home.

The bike cost $1,495 to $2,495 plus a $39/month subscription. It was aspirational — a premium product for households willing to invest in home fitness. Before the pandemic, Peloton had a loyal but niche audience. Then COVID arrived."

**[THE PANDEMIC BOOM — 1:30–2:15]**
"When gyms closed worldwide in March 2020, demand for Peloton exploded. The company couldn't manufacture bikes fast enough. Customers waited 6–8 weeks for delivery. Revenue doubled, then doubled again. The stock went from $19 at IPO to $167 by January 2021. Peloton was hiring aggressively, spending over $100 million to build new US manufacturing facilities, and expanding internationally.

CEO John Foley told employees and investors that Peloton was experiencing 'permanent demand' — that the pandemic had permanently changed how people would exercise. He was wrong."

**[THE TREADMILL RECALL — 2:15–3:15]**
"In April 2021, the US Consumer Product Safety Commission issued a public warning about Peloton's Tread+ treadmill — stating it posed a serious risk. A child had died after being pulled under a running Tread+. Multiple other injuries involving children and pets were reported.

Initially, Peloton publicly fought back against the CPSC warning — calling it 'inaccurate' and 'misleading.' This was a catastrophic PR decision. Days later, under enormous pressure, Peloton recalled 125,000 Tread+ units and later its Tread model as well. The handling of the crisis — denial before recall — became a Harvard Business School case study in crisis mismanagement."

**[THE CORRECTION — 3:15–4:00]**
"As vaccination rolled out and gyms reopened through 2021, Peloton's growth rate normalized. But the company had built its operations — its manufacturing capacity, headcount, inventory commitments — for pandemic-level demand. As demand normalized, it was left with too many bikes, too many employees, and manufacturing costs it couldn't sustain.

By December 2021, Peloton was announcing temporary production halts. The stock was crashing. Activist investors began pushing for change. A Wall Street Journal report revealed that Peloton was considering halting production of its bikes entirely."

**[CEO RESIGNATION AND PIVOT — 4:00–4:50]**
"In February 2022, John Foley resigned as CEO. He was replaced by Barry McCarthy, the former CFO of Netflix and Spotify. McCarthy immediately pivoted Peloton's strategy: from a hardware company to a fitness platform and subscription business.

Peloton began offering its bikes through rental programs, Amazon, Dick's Sporting Goods, and hotel chains. It licensed its content to other fitness hardware. It attempted to become the 'Netflix of fitness.' The business stabilized but never recaptured its pandemic heights."

**[LESSONS — 4:50–5:45]**
"Peloton is a lesson in the danger of mistaking a temporary surge for permanent demand, and of building permanent operational capacity around temporary conditions. The treadmill recall crisis added a crisis management failure to an already difficult situation.

The lesson for companies: when you're experiencing extraordinary growth, stress-test your assumptions. Ask: would this demand exist without the extraordinary circumstances? The answer for Peloton was no."

**[OUTRO — 5:45–6:00]**
"From $19 to $167 to $9. A treadmill recall. A CEO resignation. And a bike company that became a software company. Subscribe for more."
