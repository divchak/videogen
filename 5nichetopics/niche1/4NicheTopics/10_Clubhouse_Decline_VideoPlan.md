## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~880 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In early 2021, people were selling Clubhouse invitations on eBay for $100. Elon Musk hosted a room with 5,000 listeners. Oprah was dropping in. It was valued at $4 billion. Today, Clubhouse is a ghost town. No one is there. What happened to the app that was going to reinvent conversation?"

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Clubhouse launched in March 2020, created by Paul Davison and Rohan Seth at Alpha Exploration Co. Its concept was deceptively simple: audio-only chat rooms, open to the public, where anyone could listen and invited speakers could join the stage.

In a pandemic world starved of live social interaction, it caught fire instantly. The invite-only model created artificial scarcity and massive FOMO. Early adopters bragged about having Clubhouse. Tech founders, investors, journalists, celebrities — they all flooded in. In January 2021, Andreessen Horowitz led a round that valued Clubhouse at $1 billion — a unicorn in under a year."

**[THE PEAK — 1:30–2:15]**
"The first half of 2021 was Clubhouse's golden window. Elon Musk famously appeared in a Clubhouse room, and the resulting attention crashed the app with demand. Mark Zuckerberg held rooms. There were rooms about business, rooms about crypto, rooms about startups, rooms about nothing.

By February 2021, Clubhouse had 10 million weekly active users. The $4 billion valuation came shortly after. Everyone was predicting it would become the next great social platform — the audio equivalent of Twitter."

**[THE CRACKS — 2:15–3:15]**
"Then, almost in real time, the competition arrived. Twitter launched Spaces — a nearly identical feature — in May 2021. Facebook launched Live Audio Rooms. LinkedIn, Spotify, Reddit — all launched audio room features. Suddenly Clubhouse's core innovation existed everywhere, for free, inside apps people already used every day.

Clubhouse's other structural weakness was exposed: the content was live and ephemeral. There was no recording, no replay, no archive. Every conversation disappeared. You had to be there at the right moment. As novelty faded, the effort required to 'catch' good rooms stopped feeling worthwhile."

**[THE FALL — 3:15–4:15]**
"Downloads crashed through 2021. By May 2021 — just months after the February peak — monthly downloads had fallen from 9.6 million to under 1 million. By the end of 2021, Clubhouse had opened to Android users and dropped the invite-only model — two moves that stripped away the exclusivity that had made it special.

In April 2023, Clubhouse laid off 50% of its staff. The founders published a blog post about 'resetting' the product. In the blog, they acknowledged the app had stalled. Features were deprecated. The product pivoted to smaller, more intimate 'houses' — essentially admitting the original mass-room model had failed."

**[LESSONS — 4:15–5:15]**
"Clubhouse's story is a perfect case study in feature moats versus platform moats. A feature is something any company can copy. A platform is something with network effects, switching costs, and a reason to stay. Clubhouse was a feature — a great one — but it sat on its own island.

When Twitter, Facebook, Spotify, and LinkedIn copied the feature directly into their own platforms, users didn't need to switch to a separate app. The moat wasn't deep enough. The window of exclusivity was used to grow fast — but not to build something irreplaceable."

**[OUTRO — 5:15–5:30]**
"10 million users. $4 billion valuation. 18 months to ghost town status. The lesson: being first is not the same as building a sustainable business. Subscribe for more."
