## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~880 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In April 2020, two of Hollywood's most powerful people launched a billion-dollar streaming service. Six months later, it was dead. Quibi raised $1.75 billion, signed deals with the biggest names in entertainment, and launched into one of the most captive home-bound audiences in history — during a global pandemic. And still failed. Here's how."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Quibi — short for 'Quick Bites' — was the brainchild of Jeffrey Katzenberg, the legendary Hollywood producer behind DreamWorks Animation, and Meg Whitman, the former CEO of Hewlett-Packard and eBay. Their idea was simple: premium, Hollywood-quality content delivered in episodes of under 10 minutes — designed to be watched on your phone, in portrait mode, while on the go.

They raised $1.75 billion before a single episode aired. Every major studio wanted in. Every A-list celebrity signed on: Chrissy Teigen, Idris Elba, Kevin Hart, Jennifer Lopez, Liam Hemsworth. Quibi promised it was the future of entertainment."

**[THE LAUNCH — 1:30–2:30]**
"Quibi launched on April 6, 2020. Two weeks into a global lockdown. The product designed for people on the go — commuting, waiting in lines, grabbing lunch — launched into a world where nobody was going anywhere. People were at home, sitting in front of large TVs, with Netflix, Disney+, and HBO Max competing for attention.

Crucially, Quibi launched without the ability to watch on a TV. You could only watch on your phone. Users immediately complained. Katzenberg blamed the pandemic, saying the timing was 'no one's fault.' Critics disagreed."

**[THE PRODUCT PROBLEMS — 2:30–3:30]**
"Beyond the launch timing, Quibi had deep product problems. There was no social sharing feature — you couldn't screenshot or share anything from the app. In an era where TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts were growing through virality, Quibi had built an anti-viral product.

There was no free tier. $5 per month with ads, $8 without — at launch. The content, despite costing $100,000+ per minute to produce, failed to connect. Shows came and went with no word of mouth, no water cooler moment, no must-see event. The app had 1.3 million downloads on day one — but most users never converted to paid subscribers."

**[THE COLLAPSE — 3:30–4:30]**
"By August 2020, just four months after launch, reports surfaced that Quibi was exploring a sale. Nobody wanted it. Katzenberg personally called Disney, NBCUniversal, Facebook, and Apple — all passed. On October 21, 2020, just six months after launch, Quibi announced it was shutting down.

$1.75 billion spent. The content library — hundreds of hours of expensive celebrity programming — was sold off piecemeal. Most Quibi originals simply disappeared from existence."

**[THE LAWSUIT — 4:30–5:00]**
"Adding insult to injury, Quibi was sued by Eko, a startup that claimed Quibi's 'Turnstyle' feature — its signature technology that auto-rotated video between landscape and portrait mode — was stolen from their patented technology. The lawsuit ended in a settlement before the full damages could be assessed."

**[LESSONS — 5:00–5:45]**
"Quibi had money, talent, and connections. What it didn't have was a genuine consumer need. The entire premise — premium short content only on your phone — was a product built around a behavior that consumers could already satisfy for free on YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram.

No amount of celebrity content can substitute for product-market fit. And launching a 'go-somewhere' product during a global lockdown is the kind of timing mistake no amount of intelligence can overcome."

**[OUTRO — 5:45–6:15]**
"$1.75 billion. Six months. Zero cultural footprint. If you can't name a single Quibi show right now, that's the entire story. Subscribe for more."
