## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~880 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In 2019, Donald Trump visited a shuttered GM factory in Lordstown, Ohio and told auto workers their jobs were coming back. He was there to celebrate an electric truck startup called Lordstown Motors. The workers cheered. Investors poured in millions. There was just one problem: the 100,000+ truck pre-orders the company claimed were almost entirely fake."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Lordstown Motors was founded in 2018 by Steve Burns, who had previously run Workhorse Group, another EV startup. Burns purchased the shuttered General Motors Lordstown assembly plant — which had been closed in 2019, devastating the local community — for $20 million. The plan was to produce the Endurance, an electric pickup truck for commercial fleets using a revolutionary hub motor design with four in-wheel motors.

The Rust Belt setting was powerful PR. Ohio politicians backed it enthusiastically. Trump promoted it. The community needed jobs desperately. Lordstown Motors went public via SPAC in 2020, valuing it at $1.6 billion."

**[THE PRE-ORDER FRAUD — 1:30–2:30]**
"In March 2021, short-seller research firm Hindenburg Research published a damning report. It alleged that Lordstown's pre-orders — which the company had used as evidence of demand, citing 100,000+ reservations — were largely fake or non-binding. Many 'customers' turned out to be small companies with no ability to purchase the trucks, or individuals who had signed up for free with no commitment.

Hindenburg also raised concerns about the hub motor technology — suggesting it was unproven and potentially unsafe. The report triggered an SEC and DOJ investigation."

**[EXECUTIVE DEPARTURES — 2:30–3:15]**
"In June 2021, both CEO Steve Burns and CFO Julio Rodriguez resigned. This followed an internal board investigation that confirmed some of Hindenburg's findings about the pre-orders. The company disclosed that it did not have enough cash to begin production and might go bankrupt.

The SEC launched a formal investigation. Lordstown disclosed it had received subpoenas from the DOJ. The company that was supposed to bring hope back to Lordstown, Ohio was falling apart."

**[FOXCONN — THE UNLIKELY SAVIOR — 3:15–4:00]**
"In a twist, Taiwan's Foxconn — the manufacturer behind Apple's iPhones — agreed to purchase the Lordstown factory for $230 million in 2022 and signed a manufacturing partnership to produce the Endurance truck. Foxconn's investment kept Lordstown Motors alive temporarily.

The Endurance went into limited production, with a few hundred vehicles built. But quality problems, high production costs, and limited fleet demand meant the business model never proved viable. In 2023, Lordstown Motors filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy — and then sued Foxconn, claiming Foxconn had reneged on investment commitments."

**[SEC FRAUD CHARGES — 4:00–4:30]**
"In June 2023, the SEC charged Steve Burns and former CFO Julio Rodriguez with securities fraud for misleading investors about the pre-orders and production capabilities. The allegations: Burns knew the pre-orders were not genuine customer commitments but continued to represent them as evidence of robust commercial demand."

**[LESSONS — 4:30–5:15]**
"Lordstown is a story about political hype, small-town hope, and Wall Street greed converging in dangerous ways. When a narrative is too compelling — saving Rust Belt jobs, America's EV future, presidential endorsement — investors stop asking hard questions. Pre-orders with no financial commitment are not revenue. A factory is not a business. And SPACs, once again, allowed an unproven company to access public capital without the scrutiny of a traditional IPO."

**[OUTRO — 5:15–5:30]**
"100,000 fake pre-orders. A presidential endorsement. A community's hope. SEC fraud charges. Subscribe for more of the stories Wall Street didn't want to investigate first."
