## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~800 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Imagine paying $400 for a juicer that required a WiFi connection and a subscription to work — and then a journalist discovered that you could get the exact same juice by just squeezing the bag with your hands. This is the story of Juicero — Silicon Valley's most perfectly stupid product."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Juicero was founded in 2013 by Doug Evans, a passionate health entrepreneur who truly believed he was about to reinvent juice. He built a proprietary machine — sleek, heavy, WiFi-connected — that cold-pressed juice packs directly into a glass. The packs were filled with pre-chopped organic produce and delivered by subscription.

Evans pitched it as the Nespresso of juice. He called himself a 'juice shaman.' He raised $120 million from investors including Google Ventures, Kleiner Perkins — some of Silicon Valley's most respected names — along with Campbell Soup and various celebrity investors. At launch, the machine cost $700. Later it was reduced to $400."

**[THE SQUEEZE HEARD ROUND THE WORLD — 1:30–2:30]**
"In April 2017, Bloomberg journalists published a story that would destroy Juicero overnight. Their investigation found that you could achieve the exact same result as the $400 machine by simply squeezing the proprietary juice pack with your hands. No machine needed. No WiFi. No $400 investment. Just your fingers.

Juicero's response was to point out that the machine squeezed with 4 tons of force and had 400 custom parts. The internet was unimpressed. The story went globally viral. Juicero became the poster child for Silicon Valley over-engineering — spending enormous resources solving a problem that didn't exist."

**[THE SUBSCRIPTION PROBLEM — 2:30–3:15]**
"Beyond the viral squeeze story, Juicero had fundamental business model problems. The machine was heavily subsidized — sold at a loss — with the real revenue intended to come from ongoing juice pack subscriptions. But users who found that squeezing by hand worked fine simply stopped using the machine.

The packs also had expiry dates encoded in QR codes — the machine read the QR code and would refuse to press expired packs. Users were locked into the ecosystem. This level of DRM for a juice product did not go over well."

**[THE SHUTDOWN — 3:15–4:00]**
"In September 2017 — just five months after the Bloomberg story — Juicero shut down. The company had burned through its $120 million in funding. An attempt to find a buyer failed. Investors wrote off their entire positions. The machines became worthless collectibles.

Doug Evans later appeared in a documentary about the Noma restaurant and gave an interview where he described walking across Death Valley with no food for 12 days, surviving on spring water. He had moved on. His investors had not."

**[BROADER CONTEXT — 4:00–4:45]**
"Juicero wasn't alone. It represented an era of Silicon Valley 'hardware startups' that raised enormous funding to 'disrupt' everyday products — smart water bottles, connected salt shakers, WiFi-enabled toothbrushes. The logic was: if you put a chip and subscription in anything, you can achieve recurring revenue. The problem is consumers don't want a subscription for juice."

**[LESSONS — 4:45–5:30]**
"Juicero's lesson is both the simplest and most expensive in startup history: build something people actually need, not something that impresses VCs. The fact that $120 million was raised for a product that could be replaced by squeezing a bag is a perfect mirror of startup culture at its most disconnected.

Always ask before funding: what problem does this solve? For whom? And can the user do it without the product?"

**[OUTRO — 5:30–5:50]**
"$120 million. A WiFi juice machine. And two hands that did the same job for free. Subscribe — we find the stories that belong in a museum of bad ideas."
