## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~950 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"She dropped out of Stanford at 19. She wore a black turtleneck like Steve Jobs, spoke in a deep baritone, and claimed her tiny machine could run 240 blood tests from just a finger-prick. Silicon Valley loved her. The Pentagon wanted to partner with her. Walgreens put her machines in stores. There was just one problem: none of it worked."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Elizabeth Holmes founded Theranos in 2003 at age 19 with a simple, powerful vision: make blood testing cheap, accessible, and pain-free. Her technology, called the Edison device, was supposed to run hundreds of diagnostic tests from a single drop of blood, in minutes, at a fraction of the cost of traditional labs.

She surrounded herself with a legendary board: former Secretary of State George Shultz, former Secretary of Defense James Mattis, former Senators and CEOs. By 2014, Theranos was valued at $9 billion. Holmes was worth $4.5 billion on paper — the youngest female self-made billionaire in history. She was on every magazine cover."

**[THE DECEPTION — 1:30–2:30]**
"But behind the polished presentations and confident claims, the Edison machines were failing basic accuracy tests internally. They produced wildly inconsistent results. Instead of fixing the machine, Theranos quietly began running most of their tests on commercial third-party analyzers — the exact technology Holmes had promised to replace.

Employees who raised concerns were threatened with lawsuits and fired. Holmes maintained a culture of absolute secrecy, even between departments. The company was built on a lie, propped up by NDAs, lawyers, and the force of Holmes's personality."

**[THE REPORTER — 2:30–3:30]**
"Wall Street Journal reporter John Carreyrou began investigating Theranos in 2015, following tips from former employees and laboratory professionals. Holmes's legal team attacked him relentlessly — threatening lawsuits, pressuring his sources, even reportedly hiring private investigators.

But Carreyrou persisted. In October 2015, the Wall Street Journal published its bombshell investigation. Holmes appeared on CNBC the same day to deny everything, calling the report 'factually and scientifically wrong.' Most people believed her — for a while."

**[THE COLLAPSE — 3:30–4:30]**
"The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) launched an investigation. Their findings were damning: Theranos labs posed 'immediate jeopardy to patient health and safety.' They banned Holmes from operating clinical labs for two years and revoked Theranos's lab certifications. Walgreens ended its partnership. Safeway wrote off $350 million in losses.

In 2018, the SEC charged Holmes and her former partner Ramesh 'Sunny' Balwani with fraud. That same year, the US Department of Justice filed criminal charges. The $9 billion company that had never turned a profit dissolved almost entirely."

**[TRIAL & CONVICTION — 4:30–5:15]**
"The criminal trial of Elizabeth Holmes began in 2021 — one of the most watched trials of the decade. Holmes testified in her own defense, claiming Balwani had psychologically abused her. She was convicted of four counts of fraud against investors in January 2022 and sentenced to 11 years and 3 months in federal prison.

Balwani was convicted on all 12 counts and received a longer sentence. Real patients had received inaccurate blood test results — some were told they had HIV, cancer, or other conditions they didn't have. Others with real conditions may have been missed."

**[LESSONS — 5:15–6:00]**
"Theranos is a story about the danger of charisma without accountability. The board was full of military generals and politicians — not a single experienced medical or laboratory professional. No one with domain expertise was in a position to question the technology. The lesson: in healthcare, the stakes are not money — they're lives. And no amount of vision replaces actual results."

**[OUTRO — 6:00–6:30]**
"A $9 billion company. Machines that didn't work. And real patients who received false diagnoses. Subscribe — we cover the collapses that remind us why accountability matters."
