## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~820 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"On October 19, 2023, the CEO of Convoy sent an email to the company's employees. The trucking marketplace startup — which had raised $900 million and was backed by Jeff Bezos, Bill Gates, and Marc Benioff — was shutting down in two weeks. No warning. No restructuring plan. Just: we're done. This is what a $900 million failure looks like from the inside."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Convoy was founded in 2015 by Dan Lewis and Grant Goodale, both former Amazon executives. The idea was to digitize the fragmented $800 billion US trucking industry — connecting shippers who needed freight moved with independent truckers who had available capacity, using algorithms to optimize routing, pricing, and matching in real time.

The trucking industry was ripe for disruption. It was inefficient, analog, and full of middlemen. Convoy's technology genuinely worked — shippers loved the transparency, truckers appreciated better matching. The company grew quickly and raised significant funding."

**[THE BUSINESS MODEL CHALLENGE — 1:30–2:30]**
"But digital freight marketplaces operate in a brutally competitive, low-margin environment. The established players — C.H. Robinson, Echo Global Logistics, XPO — had decades of relationships, pricing power, and scale. Startups like Transfix, Uber Freight, and Amazon Freight were competing for the same shippers.

Convoy was buying freight business at thin or negative margins to gain market share — a common SaaS growth strategy that works in software but becomes extremely expensive in physical logistics where every load has real operational costs."

**[MARKET COLLAPSE — 2:30–3:15]**
"The freight market, which had boomed during COVID-era supply chain disruptions, collapsed in 2022–2023. Spot rates — the prices shippers pay for individual loads — dropped 50-60% from their pandemic peaks. For Convoy, which depended on healthy spot rates to generate margin, this was devastating.

Revenue that had grown explosively suddenly faced both falling prices and shrinking volumes. The path to profitability, which may have existed in a healthy market, became nearly impossible."

**[THE SHUTDOWN — 3:15–4:00]**
"In October 2023, facing an environment where fundraising for unprofitable logistics startups had dried up and the market showed no near-term recovery, Convoy's leadership concluded there was no viable path forward. The company had explored sale options — reportedly reaching out to potential acquirers — but found no takers at an acceptable price.

The two-week shutdown notice gave employees minimal time to find new jobs. The sudden closure was noted as particularly abrupt even by startup standards. Hundreds of employees lost jobs with almost no transition period."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"Convoy's story highlights the danger of capital-intensive growth in commodity markets. SaaS unit economics don't transfer to logistics — you can't price a truck load below cost indefinitely waiting for network effects to kick in. When the market turns and funding dries up simultaneously, even well-executed startups can collapse with almost no warning.

The two-week shutdown also raises questions about the ethical obligations of well-funded startups to their employees."

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"$900 million raised. Jeff Bezos backed. Two weeks notice. If you want to understand how SaaS thinking fails in physical industries, subscribe."
