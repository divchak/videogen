## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~800 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Tempo raised $220 million to build an AI-powered home gym that used computer vision to track your form and give real-time coaching. It was genuinely impressive technology. Then Apple released Apple Fitness+ for $9.99 a month — and Peloton's app became free — and suddenly a $2,495 machine with a $39 monthly subscription was competing against a feature on a device everyone already owned. Tempo never recovered."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Tempo was founded in 2015 by Moawia Eldeeb and Josh Augustin. After years of development, it launched in 2020 as a sleek standing studio with a large screen, built-in speakers, weights storage, and a camera that used computer vision AI to analyze your form during workouts.

The technology was genuinely differentiated: the camera could track your body position in 3D, count your reps, correct your form in real time, and dynamically adjust workout difficulty based on performance. Unlike Peloton's bike-based instruction, Tempo covered full-body strength training. The market timing — COVID lockdowns — was perfect."

**[THE FUNDING AND THE COMPETITION — 1:30–2:30]**
"Tempo raised $60 million in 2020, followed by $220 million more in 2021. At that point, the home fitness market was white-hot. Peloton's stock was near all-time highs. Mirror had been acquired by Lululemon for $500 million. The assumption was that AI-powered home fitness hardware was the future.

But the competitive picture was shifting rapidly. Apple launched Fitness+ in December 2020 — a subscription workout service that worked with the Apple Watch and required no additional hardware. Peloton launched its digital app for non-Peloton users. YouTube fitness content remained free. The premium hardware model was under attack from all sides."

**[THE PRICE PROBLEM — 2:30–3:15]**
"Tempo's biggest challenge was price elasticity in home fitness. At $2,495 for the hardware and $39/month for the subscription, Tempo was asking a significant investment from consumers who could get 90% of the fitness experience from much cheaper alternatives. Even with the form-tracking AI advantage, the premium was hard to justify at scale.

Without millions of units sold, the unit economics were difficult. Hardware margins in consumer electronics are thin. The subscription revenue needed enormous user scale to be meaningful. The company needed to grow rapidly — and it didn't."

**[THE COLLAPSE — 3:15–4:00]**
"In 2022, the home fitness market came crashing back to Earth. Gyms reopened. People stopped buying exercise equipment. Peloton's stock collapsed. Mirror was quietly wound down by Lululemon. Tempo was not immune.

By 2023, Tempo had sold its assets to iFIT Health & Fitness — the company behind NordicTrack and ProForm — for a reported fraction of its total raised capital. The AI form-tracking technology was acquired; the Tempo brand was retired. $220 million in investment yielded a distressed asset sale."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"Tempo's story is a classic example of hardware startup timing risk. The pandemic created extraordinary demand for home fitness, but that demand was artificial. Building a $220 million company on pandemic-era demand without modeling the post-pandemic correction was the fundamental error.

The Apple Fitness+ disruption was also foreseeable — when Apple enters a market with a subscription product that works on hardware you already own, standalone hardware startups face an existential challenge."

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"$220 million raised. Genuinely impressive AI. Sold for a fraction of its investment. Subscribe for more fitness for your startup knowledge."
