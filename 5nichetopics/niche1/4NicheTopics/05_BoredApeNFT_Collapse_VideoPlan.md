## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~930 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"At the peak, a cartoon picture of a bored-looking ape sold for $3.4 million. Celebrities were paying $400,000 for a JPEG. Then, as fast as it appeared, the NFT market collapsed — and those multi-million dollar apes became practically worthless. This is the story of the greatest speculative art bubble of the 21st century."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"NFTs — Non-Fungible Tokens — exploded into public consciousness in 2021. The premise was simple: using blockchain technology, you could own a unique, verified digital asset. Bored Ape Yacht Club, launched in April 2021 by a company called Yuga Labs, became the most famous collection. 10,000 cartoon apes, each slightly different, each minted for about $190.

Within months, those same apes were selling for hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars. The floor price — the minimum cost to own any ape — hit $400,000 at its peak. Celebrities jumped in: Justin Bieber, Paris Hilton, Jimmy Fallon, Eminem, Steph Curry. Owning a Bored Ape was a status symbol like no other — your proof of membership in the digital elite."

**[THE HYPE MACHINE — 1:30–2:30]**
"Yuga Labs aggressively built utility around the apes. They launched a virtual metaverse called Otherside, sold virtual land plots for hundreds of millions of dollars, and created a cryptocurrency called APE coin. The roadmap was always promising more: games, exclusive events, real-world meetups, a restaurant, a movie.

Celebrity endorsements were everywhere — but what wasn't disclosed was that many of those celebrities were being paid to promote the collections. In 2022, the SEC investigated multiple celebrities for undisclosed NFT promotions, resulting in settlements."

**[THE COLLAPSE — 2:30–3:45]**
"As interest rates rose and crypto markets fell in 2022, NFT trading volumes collapsed. The total NFT market dropped 97% from its January 2022 peak by the end of the year. Bored Apes, which had floor prices of $400,000, dropped to under $20,000 — a 95% decline.

But the collapse wasn't just market forces. Allegations surfaced that BAYC's founders had embedded white supremacist imagery in their art. A detailed investigation by blogger Ryder Ripps alleged that the Bored Ape branding contained racist dog whistles and Nazi imagery. Yuga Labs sued Ripps for defamation and won — but the controversy had permanently damaged the brand."

**[THE DOXXING AND FOUNDERS — 3:45–4:30]**
"In February 2022, journalists at Buzzfeed News doxxed the anonymous founders of Bored Ape Yacht Club — revealing they were Greg Solano and Wylie Aronson, two young men in their thirties from Miami. Yuga Labs' legal response was swift and aggressive, further damaging the community's trust that had been built around the founders' mystique."

**[METAVERSE FAILURE — 4:30–5:15]**
"The Otherside metaverse — for which Yuga Labs raised $320 million selling virtual land — became the biggest disappointment. The promised immersive gaming world delivered a technical demo that crashed the Ethereum network during launch, and years later remains largely unbuilt. Holders of virtual land that cost hundreds of thousands of dollars now hold assets worth a fraction of their purchase price."

**[LESSONS — 5:15–6:00]**
"The NFT boom was the internet equivalent of tulip mania. It was speculation dressed up as culture. The combination of celebrity hype, artificial scarcity, FOMO, and undisclosed paid promotions created a bubble that was mathematically destined to burst. The lesson: when the product is rare only because someone decided it's rare — and the only utility is reselling it to someone else — that's not an investment. That's a lottery."

**[OUTRO — 6:00–6:30]**
"A $3.4 million cartoon ape. A $320 million metaverse that never launched. And a generation of investors left holding JPEGs. Subscribe — we document the hype cycles that should have come with a warning label."
