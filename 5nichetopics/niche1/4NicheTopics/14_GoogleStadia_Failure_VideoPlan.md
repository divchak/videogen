## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~880 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In January 2023, Google sent a message to Stadia users: the service is shutting down. Millions of people had bought games, hardware, and subscriptions. Most got refunds — but the games, the progress, the communities they'd built — all of it was gone. Google had promised to reinvent gaming. Instead, it delivered one of tech's most spectacular product failures."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Google Stadia was announced at GDC — the Game Developers Conference — in March 2019 to enormous excitement. The promise was cloud gaming: no console, no downloads, just click and play any game instantly on any device with a good internet connection. Stream a 4K game at 60 frames per second from Google's data centers.

Google launched Stadia in November 2019 alongside a $130 hardware bundle — a controller and Chromecast Ultra. Subscriptions were $9.99 per month for 4K streaming, with free games. There was also a free tier. The pitch was compelling: the same Google infrastructure that powers YouTube could now power gaming."

**[THE PROBLEMS — 1:30–2:30]**
"Almost immediately, the cracks showed. Latency — the tiny delay between pressing a button and seeing it happen on screen — was noticeable to experienced gamers, even with fast internet. Gaming is deeply sensitive to latency in a way that video streaming is not.

The game library was thin. Major AAA titles took months to arrive. Developers were skeptical of investing in a platform they couldn't trust. Google's history of killing products — Google Reader, Hangouts, Inbox — made developers extremely reluctant to build Stadia-exclusive content.

And the pricing model was confusing: you still had to buy games individually even with a subscription, unlike Xbox Game Pass or PlayStation Now which offered a library."

**[THE PIVOT — 2:30–3:15]**
"In early 2021, Google made a startling announcement: they were shutting down Stadia's internal game development studios, which had been building exclusive titles. Head of Stadia gaming Jade Raymond departed. Google pivoted Stadia to a 'platform technology' business — offering cloud gaming infrastructure to other companies rather than consumers.

This was essentially an admission that the consumer product had failed. But Google kept Stadia running in consumer mode for another two years while this pivot happened — confusing users about the platform's future."

**[THE SHUTDOWN — 3:15–4:15]**
"On September 29, 2022, Google announced Stadia would shut down on January 18, 2023. Google promised full refunds for all hardware and game purchases — a surprisingly generous and consumer-friendly move. Most users received their money back.

But the damage extended beyond refunds. Game saves were lost. Online communities dissolved. Games purchased exclusively on Stadia ceased to exist. Developers who had partnered with Stadia lost their investment. The platform shut down with a library of hundreds of games that simply disappeared."

**[THE GRAVEYARD CONTEXT — 4:15–4:50]**
"Stadia joined a long list of Google products killed prematurely: Google+, Google Buzz, Google Wave, Google Glass, Google Allo, Google Daydream VR. The pattern is so well known that an entire website — 'Killed by Google' — tracks every discontinued Google service. At the time of Stadia's death, the site listed over 270 dead Google products."

**[LESSONS — 4:50–5:35]**
"Stadia failed because cloud gaming requires near-perfect internet infrastructure that most consumers don't have, because it launched without the killer content that drives platform adoption, and because developers wouldn't bet on Google given its track record of product abandonment.

The lesson for consumers: be cautious investing in platforms run by companies with a history of product abandonment — especially for libraries of content you'll lose if the service closes."

**[OUTRO — 5:35–6:00]**
"A $200 million investment. Hundreds of games. All deleted. If you enjoyed this, subscribe — we cover tech failures that cost real people real money."
