## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~880 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In August 2019, Microsoft paid an estimated $50 million to bring the world's most popular streamer, Ninja, exclusively to its platform Mixer. It was the streaming equivalent of signing LeBron James. Two years later, Mixer was shut down and Ninja had moved back to Twitch. The $50 million had purchased almost nothing."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Microsoft's Mixer was originally a company called Beam, acquired in 2016 and rebranded as Mixer in 2017. It offered streaming features Twitch didn't have — ultra-low latency streaming that allowed real-time interaction, interactive buttons, co-streaming options. The technology was genuinely innovative.

But Twitch had an insurmountable head start. Founded in 2011 and acquired by Amazon for $970 million in 2014, Twitch had 140+ million monthly active users and was the default home for gaming content. Building a competing live streaming platform meant not just matching features but convincing viewers and streamers to simultaneously move to a new place."

**[THE NINJA GAMBLE — 1:30–2:30]**
"In August 2019, Ninja — Tyler Blevins — was the biggest name in gaming. He had 14 million Twitch followers. His Fortnite streams drew millions of viewers. He had appeared on the cover of ESPN Magazine. He was the face of the gaming generation.

Microsoft's bet was clear: if the biggest streamer moves to Mixer, viewers will follow. They also signed Shroud — another mega-popular streamer — for an estimated $10+ million. The deals were the highest-profile streamer acquisitions in history. Everyone expected Mixer to surge."

**[THE VIEWERS DIDN'T FOLLOW — 2:30–3:15]**
"They didn't. Streaming audiences proved deeply loyal to platforms, not to streamers. Mixer's viewer counts didn't dramatically increase after Ninja's arrival. Ninja's own viewership on Mixer was a fraction of what he'd drawn on Twitch — the audience simply didn't make the move.

The platform had other problems: a fragmented gaming content library, limited discoverability features, and no established community culture to draw casual browsers in. Watching Twitch is a social activity as much as a content activity. Mixer felt empty by comparison."

**[COVID AND THE SHUTDOWN — 3:15–4:15]**
"COVID-19 in 2020 caused a massive surge in gaming and streaming — the biggest opportunity Mixer would ever have. Twitch grew explosively. YouTube Gaming grew. But Mixer's numbers barely moved relative to its competitors.

On June 22, 2020, Microsoft announced Mixer was shutting down in two weeks — redirecting users to Facebook Gaming in a partnership deal. Ninja's exclusive contract was released; he went back to Twitch, then moved to YouTube Gaming. Mixer was officially dead."

**[WHAT MICROSOFT MISSED — 4:15–5:00]**
"Mixer's failure reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of platform network effects. Streaming platforms are not just content — they are communities. The chat, the culture, the inside jokes, the shared history of a platform — these are not things you can buy or transplant with a single signing.

You can acquire the creator. You cannot acquire the community. Microsoft built great streaming technology but never built the community that makes a platform irreplaceable."

**[OUTRO — 5:00–5:15]**
"$50 million for Ninja. Two years. Then shutdown. The community never followed. Subscribe for more streaming post-mortems."
