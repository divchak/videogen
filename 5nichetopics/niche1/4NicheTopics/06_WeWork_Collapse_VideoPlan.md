## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~950 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"What if you could take an office rental company, give it a meditation app and a manifesto about 'elevating global consciousness,' and convince the world it was worth $47 billion? That's exactly what Adam Neumann did with WeWork — and Wall Street almost let him get away with it. Almost."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"WeWork was founded in 2010 by Adam Neumann and Miguel McKelvey. The concept was co-working spaces — rent a large floor, divide it into stylish open-plan offices, add free beer, motivational posters, and a sense of community, then sublet it to startups and freelancers for a premium.

The business model was real. Co-working was genuinely a growing trend. But Neumann didn't just want to run a real estate company. He wanted to be the next Steve Jobs. He started calling WeWork a 'technology company' and a 'physical social network.' He spoke about living forever and becoming the world's first trillionaire. Investors leaned in."

**[SOFTBANK AND THE $47B VALUATION — 1:30–2:30]**
"SoftBank's Masayoshi Son visited WeWork's New York office in 2017 and in a 12-minute tour, committed $4.4 billion to the company. Son would go on to pour a total of $18.5 billion into WeWork across multiple funding rounds. With that backing, WeWork's valuation ballooned to $47 billion — making it the most valuable startup in the United States.

Neumann used the money to expand aggressively — into 111 cities across 29 countries. He bought a $60 million Gulfstream jet on the company's tab. He owned five properties. He trademarked the word 'We' and then sold it to his own company for $5.9 million — a deal so brazen that the board forced him to return the money."

**[THE S-1 — THE BEGINNING OF THE END — 2:30–3:30]**
"In 2019, WeWork prepared to go public. To do an IPO, they had to file an S-1 document — a public disclosure of their financials. What investors saw was shocking. WeWork had lost $1.6 billion in the first half of 2019 alone. Its lease obligations exceeded $47 billion — its entire valuation — while its revenues were just $1.5 billion.

The S-1 also revealed Neumann had self-dealing structures — he personally owned buildings that WeWork rented from him. The company had never made a profit and had no clear path to profitability. Analysts began calling it what it was: a real estate company with a marketing team."

**[THE OUSTER — 3:30–4:15]**
"The IPO was shelved. WeWork's valuation cratered from $47 billion to under $10 billion in weeks. Investors demanded change. In September 2019, Adam Neumann was forced to resign as CEO. SoftBank effectively took over the company in a bailout, paying Neumann a $1.7 billion exit package — including $500 million to give up his voting shares.

A founder who ran his company into near-bankruptcy walked away with $1.7 billion. His employees, many of whom had equity in the company, got almost nothing."

**[BANKRUPTCY — 4:15–5:00]**
"Without its charismatic founder and inflated valuation, WeWork's problems only grew. COVID-19 emptied office buildings globally. Revenue collapsed. The company restructured multiple times. In November 2023, WeWork filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy — the company that was once valued at $47 billion could no longer pay its rent."

**[NEUMANN'S RETURN — 5:00–5:30]**
"In a stunning coda, in 2022 Adam Neumann raised $350 million to start a new real estate venture called Flow, with backing from Andreessen Horowitz — one of Silicon Valley's most prestigious VC firms. Silicon Valley had forgiven him almost immediately. His pitch? Basically WeWork for apartment buildings."

**[LESSONS — 5:30–6:15]**
"WeWork is the definitive story of 'fake it till you make it' taken too far. When a real estate company convinces the world it's a tech company, when a founder charges his own company for his private jet and calls it a culture investment, when a valuation is built on a dream rather than a business model — the only question is not if, but when."

**[OUTRO — 6:15–6:30]**
"$47 billion. A trademarked word. A $1.7 billion exit for the man who broke the company. And a return to fundraising 12 months later. Subscribe — this stuff keeps happening."
