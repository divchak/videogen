## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (~820 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Fast.com raised $120 million to build a one-click checkout button. They were burning $10 million per month. When they shut down in April 2022, they had generated just $600,000 in revenue — ever. The company had spent 200 times its total revenue in a single month of operations. This is a story about startup spending culture taken to its most absurd extreme."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Fast was founded in 2019 by Domm Holland, a young Australian entrepreneur, with the mission of eliminating friction from online checkout. The vision was genuinely useful: create a universal login that autofills your payment and shipping information across any participating merchant, with a single click. No creating accounts. No filling forms. Just click Fast and you're done.

The concept competed directly with Shop Pay (from Shopify), Apple Pay, Google Pay, and PayPal's various one-click checkout solutions — all of which were backed by trillion-dollar platform companies. Fast raised $20 million in seed funding in 2020 and then $102 million more in 2021 from Stripe and others."

**[THE SPENDING PROBLEM — 1:30–2:30]**
"The $120 million was spent with extraordinary speed. Fast built an opulent San Francisco office. It paid extremely high salaries and generous benefits. It sponsored major events. The company had around 400 employees at its peak — for a product that had not yet demonstrated commercial traction.

Reports from former employees described a culture where spending was never questioned. The assumption was that revenue would come as the product scaled. The question that was not asked frequently enough: why would merchants choose Fast over Stripe, PayPal, Apple Pay, and Shopify — all of which already had enormous installed bases and zero-friction checkout solutions?"

**[THE REVENUE REALITY — 2:30–3:15]**
"When the numbers emerged at shutdown, they were staggering. Fast had been generating approximately $600,000 in total lifetime revenue — meaning tens of thousands of dollars per month at best — while spending $10 million monthly. The ratio of burn to revenue was extraordinary.

The underlying problem: the two-sided marketplace challenge. Fast needed merchants to integrate its checkout to attract consumers. But consumers needed to use Fast with many merchants to justify installing it. Neither side scaled to the critical mass needed."

**[THE SHUTDOWN — 3:15–4:00]**
"In April 2022, Fast announced it was shutting down. CEO Domm Holland sent a message to employees, who were reportedly given little warning. The shutdown was rapid. The company had reached the end of its funding and had no viable path to raising more.

Stripe — one of Fast's major investors — had a direct conflict of interest: Stripe Checkout was a direct competitor. The strategic rationale for Stripe's investment has been questioned by industry observers."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"Fast teaches a crucial lesson about the difference between fundraising success and business success. Raising $120 million is an achievement — but it's not validation that your product will work. The competitive landscape question — why would customers choose this over incumbent solutions? — needs to be answered before spending $10 million per month.

Spending at scale before achieving product-market fit doesn't accelerate success. It accelerates the path to shutdown."

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"$120 million raised. $600,000 in lifetime revenue. $10 million burned per month. Subscribe for more startup math that doesn't add up."
