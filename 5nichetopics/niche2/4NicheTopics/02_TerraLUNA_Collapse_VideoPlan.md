## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,100 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"What if I told you that $60 billion — not stolen, not hacked, not misplaced — simply stopped existing? Not gradually. Not over years. In four days. In May 2022, the Terra/LUNA ecosystem went through a death spiral so fast and so total it became the financial equivalent of a building evaporating mid-air. Sixty billion dollars. Gone. And the man who built it had publicly mocked anyone who said this could happen. So let's talk about that man."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Terra was built by Do Kwon — a Stanford-educated South Korean developer who radiated the kind of confidence that either makes you want to invest everything you own, or run in the opposite direction. His flagship product was UST, a so-called 'algorithmic stablecoin' — always worth exactly one US dollar.

But here's the key word: algorithmic. Unlike USDC or Tether, UST was not backed by real dollars sitting in a bank. Instead, it used a second token called LUNA and a mathematical mechanism — burn UST to mint LUNA, burn LUNA to mint UST — to maintain that $1 value through pure supply-and-demand logic. Elegant on paper. Do Kwon called skeptics 'poor.' He publicly tweeted that 95% of crypto projects would fail — just not his. He was, it turned out, half right."

**[THE ANCHOR PROTOCOL — 1:30–2:30]**
"The rocket fuel powering UST's explosive growth was a platform called Anchor Protocol. And Anchor was offering something that made every finance professional's eyebrows slowly climb toward the ceiling: a guaranteed 20% annual yield on UST deposits. For reference — your savings account pays maybe 0.5%. A good index fund returns 7–10% over a decade. Anchor was paying 20%. Guaranteed. Like clockwork.

Billions poured in. At its peak, Anchor held $17 billion in UST deposits. Regular people — nurses, teachers, retirees — treated it like a savings account. Because that's exactly what Anchor marketed it as. The question nobody asked loudly enough: where is that 20% actually coming from? The answer, it turned out, was a reserves fund draining by hundreds of millions every single month. The yield was a subsidy. A very expensive one with a very limited timeline."

**[THE ATTACK — 2:30–3:45]**
"In early May 2022, someone — identity still publicly unknown — started making moves. Massive UST withdrawals from Anchor. Simultaneous large dumps of UST on exchanges. The scale was engineered to do one specific thing: knock UST below its $1 peg. And it worked. UST dropped to $0.98. Then $0.95. The algorithm activated, minting billions of new LUNA tokens to restore balance. But the selling pressure was overwhelming the mechanism.

And here's where it became an irreversible death spiral. More LUNA was minted to defend UST — which flooded the market — which crashed LUNA's price — which meant each LUNA was worth less as collateral — which meant even more LUNA needed to be printed — which crashed it further. A self-reinforcing loop with no exit. In 72 hours, LUNA fell from $80 to $0.0002. That is not a typo. Eighty dollars to two one-hundred-thousandths of a cent. UST fell to $0.10. Sixty billion dollars in combined market cap was simply deleted."

**[THE HUMAN COST — 3:45–4:45]**
"Then the human news arrived. Not financial news — human news. Korean media reported suicides. Online forums filled with people describing what they'd lost. A nurse in the US — $50,000 gone. A retiree in Canada using UST as a pension substitute because 20% felt safer than a volatile stock market. A young couple in Southeast Asia whose entire financial plan dissolved in 72 hours. Do Kwon had specifically marketed UST as a 'savings account for the unbanked.' The people who trusted that marketing the most were the ones who could least afford the loss. And Do Kwon's first public response after the collapse? A tweet saying he was 'not worried.' It later emerged he had withdrawn significant personal funds before the collapse began."

**[DO KWON — FUGITIVE — 4:45–5:30]**
"South Korean authorities issued an arrest warrant in September 2022. Interpol issued a Red Notice — a global request for arrest and extradition. For months, Do Kwon continued tweeting from an unknown location, insisting he was 'not on the run.' Which is technically true — if you define 'not on the run' as 'hiding somewhere and telling journalists you're not hiding.' In March 2023, he was caught in Montenegro trying to board a flight using falsified Costa Rican documents. In 2024, he was extradited to the US to face federal fraud charges. The man who called you poor for questioning him is now explaining himself to a federal judge."

**[LESSONS — 5:30–6:15]**
"Here is what Terra's collapse permanently added to the financial vocabulary: if something promises 20% guaranteed returns, someone is absorbing that risk somewhere — and that someone is eventually you. The word 'stable' in stablecoin is marketing, not engineering, unless real assets back it. Algorithmic systems fail in ways designers didn't model, because humans are naturally better at optimism than at stress-testing. And founders who loudly mock critics are, by historical record, almost always hiding something. The louder the mockery, the bigger the secret."

**[OUTRO — 6:15–6:45]**
"Sixty billion dollars. Four days. A founder who called you poor — now answering questions in a courtroom. If you think this is a crypto-only problem, wait until you see how many of the next 29 stories follow the exact same playbook. Subscribe — we cover these collapses so you recognize the pattern before it costs you."
