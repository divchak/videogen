## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~950 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"What if you put robots in a truck, baked the pizza while driving to your door, and called it the future of food? Then, when that didn't work out, pivoted to sustainable packaging. Then to automated food trucks. Then to drone delivery. Then to something else entirely. Zume Pizza raised $375 million from SoftBank, went through at least five distinct strategic identities, and still failed to build a single successful business. In the annals of startup history, Zume stands alone — not just for failing, but for the sheer creative persistence with which it invented new ways to fail."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Zume Pizza was founded in 2015 by Alex Garden and Julia Collins in Mountain View, California. The original innovation was legitimate: robot arms in a central kitchen would pre-assemble custom pizzas, and then a second set of robots in delivery trucks would actually bake the pizzas while in transit — so the pizza arrived fresh out of the oven at the exact moment of delivery. No sitting in a warming bag getting soggy. No cold crust.

It solved a real problem. Pizza quality degrades rapidly after baking. Zume's model attacked that problem directly. The engineering required was complex but not impossible. The concept attracted attention — and when Masayoshi Son of SoftBank noticed something interesting, he acted fast."

**[SOFTBANK MAKES IT WORSE — 1:30–2:15]**
"In November 2018, SoftBank invested $375 million in Zume at approximately a $2.25 billion valuation. This is Masayoshi Son's signature move: identify a company with an interesting idea, and pour in capital at a scale that forces rapid, ambitious scaling. The theory: more money means faster growth means dominant market position. The problem: more money than a company's operational reality can absorb doesn't produce faster growth. It produces organizational chaos.

Zume hired aggressively — growing to over 1,400 employees. It began pursuing a vision far beyond pizza: an entire food-as-a-service platform, with automated kitchens and trucks that could make any food on demand, anywhere. Each new direction required new R&D, new supply chain relationships, new regulatory navigation. The $375 million that could have made one focused company excellent was instead funding a sprawling experiment with no clear center."

**[THE PIVOT PARADE — 2:15–3:15]**
"Pizza delivery via robot truck faced economics that didn't scale, regulatory complexity in multiple cities, and a competitive landscape full of established players. So Zume pivoted. To sustainable packaging — designing compostable, plant-based food packaging sold to other restaurants. It was a completely different business from pizza robots. Then it pivoted to licensing its kitchen automation technology to restaurant brands. Then it explored drone delivery. Then smart home cooking appliances. Then back to packaging full-time.

Each pivot required abandoning the work, team, and infrastructure of the previous direction. Each one burned money. And each one was announced with a press release that tried to make strategic repositioning sound like exciting evolution."

**[THE COLLAPSE — 3:15–4:00]**
"In January 2020, Zume laid off approximately 360 employees — roughly half the company — and announced it was shutting down the pizza delivery business entirely. The robot pizza trucks, which had been the company's entire founding identity, were auctioned off. The company then tried to survive on packaging and food technology licensing, but those businesses never generated meaningful revenue.

In 2023, the packaging business also collapsed. Zume shut down completely. SoftBank wrote off its $375 million investment. The robot pizza trucks were sold. Alex Garden and Julia Collins moved on to new ventures. The company that was going to transform food delivery has a footnote in the history of SoftBank's Vision Fund losses."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"Zume is a case study in what happens when capital is used as a substitute for business model validation. The pizza robot concept had genuine merit at a small scale — but the unit economics of food delivery are unforgiving, and the investment should have been sized to prove the model before betting $375 million on it. Once the SoftBank money arrived, the pressure to grow beyond pizza — to become the food-as-a-service platform that justified a $2 billion valuation — pulled the company far from what it was actually good at.

Too much money, too soon, from an investor whose playbook doesn't distinguish between software scale and physical operations scale, can be as fatal as too little capital."

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"$375 million. Robot pizza trucks. Five pivots. Zero successful businesses. The lesson here practically tells itself. Subscribe — we document the most instructive failures in startup history."
