## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~950 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Tempo raised $220 million to build an AI-powered home gym that used a camera and computer vision to track your body in 3D, count your reps, correct your form in real time, and coach you through strength training workouts. The technology was genuinely impressive. The timing was perfect — peak pandemic home fitness demand. Then Apple launched Fitness+ for $9.99 a month, Peloton's app went free, and gyms reopened. By 2023, Tempo had sold its assets to iFIT at a fraction of its total raised capital. This is what happens when a great product launches into a market that's about to collapse and a competitive landscape that's about to get a lot more expensive."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Tempo was founded in 2015 by Moawia Eldeeb and Josh Augustin. The product took years to develop: a standing studio unit with a large touchscreen, built-in speakers, storage for weights, and — most importantly — a camera that used computer vision to track the user's body position in three dimensions. The AI could see you. It could count reps. It could detect poor form and correct it immediately. It could adjust workout difficulty based on your performance in real time.

This was genuinely different from anything else in home fitness. Peloton's model was instruction-based: watch an instructor, follow along. Tempo's model was interactive: the machine watches you and adapts to you. The hardware cost $1,995 to $2,495 plus a $39 monthly subscription. Investors who saw the demo were impressed — $60 million was raised in 2020, and $220 million more came in 2021 as the home fitness market was white-hot."

**[THE COVID DEMAND AND COMPETITORS — 1:30–2:30]**
"COVID-19 created the greatest home fitness demand surge in history. Gyms were closed. People were at home. The exercise equipment market grew by over 170% in 2020. Peloton's stock hit $167. Mirror was acquired by Lululemon for $500 million. It looked like home fitness hardware was the future of exercise permanently.

But in December 2020, Apple launched Apple Fitness+. For $9.99 a month — or free with certain Apple Watch purchases — you got guided workouts across every category, with instructors who appeared to move in sync with your music and your Apple Watch data. No new hardware needed. Just the device 1.5 billion people already owned. For someone evaluating whether to spend $2,495 on a Tempo, Apple Fitness+ was a devastating alternative to present at exactly the wrong moment."

**[THE POST-PANDEMIC CORRECTION — 2:30–3:15]**
"As vaccines rolled out through 2021 and gyms reopened, home fitness demand normalized with remarkable speed. The 170% growth of 2020 reversed. Exercise equipment sales fell sharply. Peloton collapsed. Mirror was wound down by Lululemon. The entire category corrected simultaneously, and Tempo — with its $2,495 price point and 10-figure VC backing — was not immune.

The company had built its operational scale — manufacturing partnerships, software development, customer support infrastructure — for pandemic-level demand. When that demand evaporated, the unit economics became even more challenging. Hardware is unforgiving: you have inventory, you have manufacturing commitments, and you cannot easily reduce costs when demand falls."

**[THE ACQUISITION — 3:15–4:00]**
"In 2023, Tempo sold its assets to iFIT Health & Fitness — the company behind NordicTrack and ProForm home gym equipment. The sale was for an undisclosed amount. Given that Tempo had raised $220 million, and the sale was not announced with any financial celebration, the math was clearly unfavorable for investors. The Tempo brand was retired. The technology — the computer vision form tracking system — was acquired, potentially to be integrated into iFIT's future product lines.

For the founders and team, the outcome was painful but not without value: the technology they built was genuinely novel, and it found a new home with a company that has the distribution and manufacturing scale to potentially bring it to broader market."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"Tempo's story combines two major risks that consumer hardware startups always face: platform disruption and demand cyclicality. When a platform company — Apple — builds a competing product that requires no additional hardware, the addressable market for your hardware shrinks immediately and permanently. And when your core market demand was driven by an extraordinary external event — a pandemic — the return to baseline is not a setback. It's the correction of a temporary illusion.

The lesson for hardware startup investing: platform risk and cyclical demand risk are not hypothetical. They are the two most common ways consumer hardware companies die. Model for them explicitly before betting $220 million on the outcome."

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"$220 million raised. Computer vision that could watch you work out. Apple Fitness+ at $9.99. Gyms reopened. Assets sold. Subscribe — we cover what the pitch decks didn't model."
