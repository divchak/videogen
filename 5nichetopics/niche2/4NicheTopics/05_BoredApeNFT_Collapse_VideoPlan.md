## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,050 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"At the peak of the frenzy, a cartoon picture of a bored-looking ape sold for $3.4 million. Not a painting. Not a sculpture. A JPEG. A digital file that anyone could right-click and save to their desktop in seconds. Celebrities were paying $400,000 just to get into the club. The entire NFT market was valued at tens of billions. And then — as reliably as every bubble before it — it popped. The apes didn't move. The value did. And it moved all the way down."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Bored Ape Yacht Club launched in April 2021 — created by Yuga Labs, founded by two young men from Miami using pseudonyms. The collection: 10,000 cartoon apes, each slightly different, each minted as an NFT for about $190. That's it. That was the product. A profile picture you could use on Twitter. The NFT technology meant you 'owned' a unique verified version. The ownership came with membership in an exclusive club — and in 2021, exclusivity was worth a fortune.

Within months, the floor price — the minimum you'd pay for any ape — hit $400,000. The secondary market was exploding. Then the celebrities arrived. Justin Bieber paid $1.3 million for his. Paris Hilton, Jimmy Fallon, Eminem, Steph Curry, Post Malone — all proudly displayed their apes as profile pictures on Twitter. Owning a Bored Ape wasn't just an investment. It was the velvet rope of the internet. And the line to get past that rope was very, very long."

**[THE HYPE MACHINE — 1:30–2:30]**
"Yuga Labs was smart about building additional value around the apes. They launched a cryptocurrency called APE coin. They sold virtual land in a digital metaverse called Otherside — raising $320 million in a single day. The roadmap was always promising more: exclusive events, a real-world restaurant, a movie, a game. The community was passionate, evangelical, and spending heavily.

But what nobody was talking about loudly enough was this: many of those celebrity endorsements weren't organic enthusiasm. They were paid promotions — and not disclosed as such. In 2022, the SEC investigated and fined multiple celebrities for promoting NFT collections without clearly stating they were being paid. Kim Kardashian paid $1.26 million to settle a related SEC case. The hype machine, it turned out, had an invoice attached to it."

**[THE COLLAPSE — 2:30–3:45]**
"As interest rates rose globally through 2022 and the broader crypto market fell sharply, NFT trading volume fell off a cliff. Not a gentle decline — a cliff. Total NFT market trading volume dropped by 97% from its January 2022 peak by year's end. Ninety-seven percent. If you'd bought a Bored Ape at peak for $400,000, you were now looking at a floor price under $20,000 — a 95% decline. That $3.4 million ape? Worth roughly what you'd pay for a decent used car.

Making things worse: a detailed investigation by artist Ryder Ripps alleged that Bored Ape's branding contained racist symbolism and imagery inspired by far-right iconography. Yuga Labs sued Ripps for defamation and ultimately won. But the lawsuit itself — aggressive, expensive, targeting a critic — damaged the community's trust in the founders and kept the controversy in headlines for over a year."

**[THE DOXXING AND OTHERSIDE — 3:45–4:30]**
"In February 2022, BuzzFeed News published the identities of Bored Ape's anonymous founders — Greg Solano and Wylie Aronson, two thirty-something men from Miami. Yuga Labs responded with legal threats to BuzzFeed reporters. For a community that had been built around founder mystique, the public legal aggression felt like a betrayal.

And then there was Otherside — the metaverse Yuga Labs had raised $320 million to build. Its launch event was a technical disaster that congested the Ethereum network so severely that users paid millions in failed transaction fees. Years later, the 'immersive gaming world' remains largely unbuilt, and those virtual land parcels that cost hundreds of thousands of dollars are worth a fraction of their purchase price."

**[LESSONS — 4:30–5:15]**
"The Bored Ape story is the clearest modern example of what economists call a speculative bubble — where price is driven not by underlying value, but by the expectation that someone will pay even more later. The combination of artificial scarcity, celebrity hype, undisclosed paid promotions, and an entire community incentivized to keep prices rising created a self-reinforcing loop. Until it didn't.

When the only utility of an asset is reselling it to someone else at a higher price, you are not investing. You are playing musical chairs. The music stopped in 2022. A lot of people were standing when it did."

**[OUTRO — 5:15–5:30]**
"$3.4 million for a cartoon ape. $320 million for a metaverse that doesn't exist. And a market that dropped 97%. The internet's most expensive profile picture collection — fully documented right here. Subscribe."
