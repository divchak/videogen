## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,050 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In 2016, Twitter shut down Vine — the six-second video app that invented the format TikTok is now worth $100 billion on. Twitter had bought Vine before it even launched. They owned the future of short-form video for four years. And they let it die because they wouldn't pay creators. Two years later, TikTok launched the exact same concept and conquered the world. This is the most expensive strategic mistake in social media history — and it happened in slow motion, in public, while the whole industry watched."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Vine launched in January 2013, just weeks after Twitter acquired it for an estimated $30 million — before a single video was uploaded. The concept was deceptively simple: six seconds of looping video. That constraint turned out to be the product. The limitation forced creativity in a way unlimited time never could. You had six seconds to make someone laugh, think, react — and loop. Comedy found it first. Then musicians. Then teenagers. Then everyone.

By 2015, Vine had over 200 million registered users. The stars who built their careers there — Vine names like Logan Paul, Lele Pons, King Bach, Shawn Mendes — would become some of the biggest celebrities of the following decade. The format was new, native to mobile, and deeply engaging. For a brief, brilliant window, Vine was the most creatively exciting platform on the internet."

**[TWITTER'S NEGLECT — 1:30–2:30]**
"But Twitter treated Vine like a stepchild — present but underinvested. There was minimal product development. There was no mechanism to monetize the creators building the platform's entire value. YouTube creators were earning real money through ad revenue splits. Vine creators were earning nothing from the platform itself — only from brand deals they negotiated entirely independently.

Meanwhile, Instagram introduced video. Snapchat launched Stories. YouTube was growing aggressively. The competitive environment was moving fast, and Vine was standing still. When creators — who were building tens of millions of followers on the platform — raised the issue of monetization, Twitter didn't build it. The people who were Vine's product were left to figure out their own income."

**[THE CREATOR ULTIMATUM — 2:30–3:15]**
"In 2016, a group of Vine's top creators — who collectively commanded hundreds of millions of followers — approached Vine management with a direct business proposal: pay each of us $1.2 million in exchange for a commitment to keep creating content on the platform. The math wasn't unreasonable. These creators were Vine's reason for existing. Without them, the platform had no audience. The ask was: treat us like the asset we actually are.

Vine said no. The creators began planning exits. Some moved to YouTube. Some moved to Instagram. And shortly after, Twitter — facing its own existential financial pressures, having rejected acquisition offers from Google, Disney, and Salesforce — began cutting costs. Vine was in the crosshairs."

**[THE DEATH — 3:15–4:15]**
"On October 27, 2016, Twitter announced Vine would shut down within months. The closure was swift and brutal. What hadn't been anticipated was the archival problem: billions of six-second videos — an enormous archive of internet culture from 2013 to 2016 — was at risk of disappearing permanently. A team of archivists at the Internet Archive scrambled to preserve as much as possible. Some of it survived. Much didn't.

The creators scattered. The audience moved to YouTube, Instagram, Snapchat — and waited for the next short-video platform. That platform arrived two years later from China, and it was called TikTok."

**[THE IRONY — 4:15–5:00]**
"In 2018, TikTok launched in Western markets. Its format: short looping videos, optimized for mobile, with strong algorithmic discovery. Sound familiar? TikTok reached 1 billion monthly active users in 2021 — a feat that took Facebook years. Instagram responded with Reels. YouTube responded with Shorts. The entire short-form video economy, which is now worth hundreds of billions of dollars in aggregate, is built on the format that Vine pioneered and Twitter threw away. Jack Dorsey later publicly called shutting down Vine one of his biggest regrets."

**[LESSONS — 5:00–5:45]**
"Vine is a masterclass in the cost of not investing in creators. Platforms are only as good as the content on them. Content is only as good as the incentives you give the creators making it. If you extract value from creators without sharing it, they will leave — because they can. The window of competitive advantage that Vine had — the years between 2013 and 2016 when it owned short video — was a gift. Twitter didn't invest in it. Someone else was watching, and they took the idea and built a trillion-dollar category."

**[OUTRO — 5:45–6:00]**
"$30 million acquisition. 200 million users. Refused to pay creators $1.2 million. Shut down in 2016. TikTok is now worth $100 billion doing the same thing. Subscribe — we document the decisions that changed the internet."
