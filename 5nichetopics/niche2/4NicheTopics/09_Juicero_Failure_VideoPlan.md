## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~950 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In 2017, a Silicon Valley startup raised $120 million from Google Ventures and Kleiner Perkins — two of the most respected names in venture capital — to build a WiFi-connected juice press that cost $400. Then Bloomberg journalists discovered that you could achieve the exact same result by simply squeezing the bag with your hands. No machine. No WiFi. No $400. Just your fingers. And suddenly, one startup became the symbol of everything wrong with how Silicon Valley thinks about problems."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Juicero was founded in 2013 by Doug Evans, a health entrepreneur with genuine passion for cold-pressed juice and a flair for startup mythology. He called himself a 'juice shaman.' He once compared himself to Steve Jobs — with a completely straight face. His vision: democratize access to cold-pressed juice, the expensive boutique product, by bringing it into the home. The delivery mechanism would be proprietary produce packs — pre-portioned, pre-chopped, QR-code encrypted, subscription-based. And a machine to press them.

The pitch worked brilliantly. Google Ventures wrote a check. Kleiner Perkins wrote a check. Campbell Soup invested. Various celebrity investors joined. At launch, the machine retailed at $700 — later reduced to $400 after public outcry. The business model was elegant in theory: sell the machine at a loss, make the real money on the ongoing juice pack subscriptions. It was the Nespresso model, applied to produce. Nespresso makes billions. Juicero did not."

**[THE SQUEEZE HEARD ROUND THE WORLD — 1:30–2:30]**
"April 2017. Bloomberg reporters Ellen Huet and Olivia Zaleski received a tip and decided to test a very simple hypothesis: what happens if you just squeeze the juice pack with your hands instead of using the $400 machine? They filmed themselves doing it. The result: the same juice came out. Same quality. Same speed. No machine required. They published the story with the video.

The internet detonated. Juicero became the meme of the year, the symbol of Silicon Valley's detachment from reality. Every tech joke for months referenced the juice machine. Juicero's official response was both true and completely missing the point: they explained that the machine applied 4 tons of force and had over 400 custom-engineered parts. All absolutely correct. All completely irrelevant to the fact that your hands achieved the same outcome."

**[THE SUBSCRIPTION TRAP — 2:30–3:15]**
"Beyond the viral PR disaster, Juicero had a deeper structural problem that the Bloomberg story merely exposed rather than created. The entire revenue model depended on customers buying ongoing juice pack subscriptions — around $5 to $10 per pack. But the packs had QR codes that the machine scanned to verify freshness. Expired packs wouldn't be pressed. And once customers discovered that squeezing by hand worked fine, many simply used the machine to store the packs and squeezed manually — buying the subscriptions while bypassing the machine entirely.

The company had also locked itself into an expensive produce supply chain, a complex proprietary delivery logistics system, and a hardware product that required significant ongoing support. Every part of the operation was expensive. Almost none of it was generating the revenue required to sustain it."

**[THE SHUTDOWN — 3:15–4:00]**
"In September 2017 — just five months after the Bloomberg story — Juicero announced it was shutting down. The company had burned through its $120 million. An attempted sale process found no interested buyers. The machines became instant collectibles and eBay oddities. Doug Evans resurfaced in various wellness documentaries, including one about extreme fasting in Death Valley, and eventually moved on to his next venture.

The investors wrote off their positions. The employees found other jobs. And Juicero entered the permanent hall of fame of startup failures — the rare case where the product's core problem can be demonstrated in a 10-second video clip that anyone can understand immediately."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"Juicero teaches what no MBA program can fully capture in theory: the difference between a solution that impresses investors in a conference room and a solution that creates genuine value in someone's kitchen. The question that was never rigorously asked: what does the machine do that the customer cannot do themselves, simply? When the answer is nothing, you don't have a product. You have a very expensive prop.

The $120 million raised for Juicero also shows how a compelling narrative — 'democratize cold-pressed juice, build a connected kitchen ecosystem, Nespresso for produce' — can substitute for basic product validation. Always ask: does the customer need the machine, or do they just need the outcome the machine promises?"

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"$120 million. Google Ventures. A WiFi juicer. Two hands that did the same job for free. Subscribe — we archive the most instructive mistakes in startup history."
