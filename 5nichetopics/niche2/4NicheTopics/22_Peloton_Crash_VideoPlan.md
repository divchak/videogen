## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,050 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"In January 2021, Peloton's stock hit $167 per share. The connected exercise bike was the hottest consumer product in America. Hundreds of thousands of people were on waiting lists. Instructors were celebrities. The company had expanded into treadmills and was building a $400 million factory in Ohio. Then: a child's death. A mishandled product recall that the company initially denied needed to happen. A CEO who had told investors they were experiencing 'permanent demand.' The stock fell 94 percent. And a company worth $50 billion learned that pandemic demand is not permanent — it's borrowed from the future."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Peloton was founded in 2012 by John Foley. The product was simple and genuinely excellent: a premium stationary bike with a large touchscreen that streamed live and on-demand cycling classes with energetic, personality-driven instructors. The experience was designed to replicate the feel and motivation of a boutique fitness studio — from home.

The bike cost between $1,495 and $2,495, plus a $39 monthly subscription. Before the pandemic, Peloton was a successful niche product — beloved by a specific, affluent audience, but far from mainstream. Its IPO in September 2019 valued the company at around $8 billion. Modest growth was expected. Then March 2020 happened, and gyms closed worldwide overnight."

**[THE PANDEMIC BOOM — 1:30–2:15]**
"With every gym in the world closed, Peloton's phones exploded. The company couldn't manufacture bikes fast enough. Delivery wait times stretched to 6 to 8 weeks. Revenue doubled, then doubled again. Stock went from under $20 to $167 by January 2021. Peloton was hiring aggressively, acquiring the fitness equipment company Precor for $420 million, spending $400 million to build a new US manufacturing facility, and expanding internationally.

CEO John Foley began making statements about 'permanent demand' — the idea that the pandemic had durably changed how people exercised, and that home fitness's massive growth was structural rather than cyclical. He was publicly and genuinely convinced. He was wrong in a way that would cost the company billions."

**[THE TREADMILL RECALL — 2:15–3:15]**
"In April 2021, the US Consumer Product Safety Commission issued a public safety warning about Peloton's Tread+ treadmill. The CPSC stated that it posed a serious risk of injury to children and pets who could be pulled under the running belt. A child had died. Multiple other injuries had been reported. The CPSC was urging all Peloton Tread+ owners with children or pets to stop using the machine immediately.

Peloton's initial response was to fight back — publicly. The company called the CPSC warning 'inaccurate and misleading.' CEO John Foley released a statement saying Peloton 'believes the Tread+ is safe.' Days later, under enormous public and regulatory pressure, Peloton reversed course and announced a voluntary recall of both the Tread+ and its standard Tread treadmill — 125,000 units in total. The sequence — deny, then recall — became a case study taught in business schools on how not to handle a product safety crisis."

**[THE CORRECTION — 3:15–4:00]**
"As vaccines rolled out through 2021 and gyms reopened, Peloton's growth rate normalized with remarkable speed. But the company had built its entire operational structure — headcount, manufacturing capacity, supplier commitments — for pandemic-level demand. As demand returned to pre-pandemic baselines, Peloton had thousands of unsold bikes sitting in warehouses and manufacturing lines set up to produce far more than anyone was buying.

By December 2021, a bombshell Wall Street Journal report revealed that Peloton was considering temporarily halting production of its bikes entirely because of collapsing demand. That single story destroyed whatever remained of investor confidence. The stock, already falling, accelerated into a collapse."

**[CEO RESIGNATION AND PIVOT — 4:00–4:50]**
"In February 2022, John Foley resigned as CEO. He was replaced by Barry McCarthy — the former CFO of both Netflix and Spotify, and a seasoned operator at platform-transition companies. McCarthy immediately pivoted Peloton's strategy: instead of a hardware company, Peloton would become a fitness platform. Bikes and treadmills through rental programs. App available without hardware. Distribution through Amazon and Dick's Sporting Goods. Content licensing to hotel chains and gyms.

The pivot made strategic sense. But executing a business model transformation while the underlying brand was damaged and the market had moved on is among the hardest things a company can do. Peloton's financials stabilized — but its stock never recaptured its peak, and McCarthy himself stepped down in 2024."

**[LESSONS — 4:50–5:45]**
"Peloton is a case study in two simultaneous failure modes. First: the danger of extrapolating extraordinary demand without testing whether it will persist when circumstances return to normal. Second: the cost of mishandling a product safety crisis — specifically, the decision to publicly deny a safety concern before reversing course. That sequence of denial and then reversal damaged trust in a way that the recall itself would not have.

The larger lesson: when your business is growing 200% because of a once-in-a-generation global event, the correct question is: how much of this growth would exist in a normal world? If the answer is 'much less,' your operational decisions should reflect that."

**[OUTRO — 5:45–6:00]**
"$50 billion at peak. A child's death. A recall that was initially denied. A stock that fell 94%. And a CEO who believed in permanent demand. Subscribe — we document the moment the market corrected the narrative."
