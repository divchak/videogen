## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,050 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Two childhood friends borrowed billions of dollars from hundreds of companies and then simply — disappeared. On a yacht. Three Arrows Capital was the most powerful, most trusted name in crypto investing. Every serious player in the space knew their reputation. And in June 2022, they collectively learned that a reputation is not a balance sheet. $18 billion in assets. Gone. And the founders? Somewhere on a $50 million superyacht they'd ordered while the whole thing was burning down. You could not write this."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Three Arrows Capital — 3AC — was founded in 2012 by Su Zhu and Kyle Davies, two Singaporeans who'd been friends since prep school and had previously worked as traders at Credit Suisse. They started in forex trading and, like everyone else, gradually migrated into crypto. Unlike everyone else, they were early — and they were very good at picking winners. Early positions in Ethereum, Avalanche, and dozens of other projects turned into enormous returns.

By 2021, 3AC was estimated to manage $18 billion in assets. They weren't just investors — they were kingmakers. If Su Zhu tweeted that a project was interesting, the price moved. If 3AC backed your protocol, it was legitimized overnight. Their Twitter presence was considered authoritative. Their reputation was bulletproof. Every major crypto lender wanted to be in business with them. That reputation, as it turned out, was the only collateral that mattered — because it was all they really had."

**[THE STRATEGY — 1:30–2:30]**
"3AC's core strategy was deceptively simple and deeply dangerous: borrow as much as possible from as many lenders as possible, invest it all in crypto, and profit when the market goes up. They had credit lines with Voyager Digital, BlockFi, Genesis Capital, Babel Finance, and multiple others — borrowing hundreds of millions from each, independently, simultaneously.

Here's the critical problem: none of those lenders knew how much 3AC had borrowed from the others. In traditional finance, credit bureaus exist precisely to prevent this. In crypto in 2021, there were no credit bureaus. Lenders were blinded by reputation and the fact that everyone trusted 3AC. They skipped the due diligence because 3AC seemed like they had already been due-diligenced by everyone else. Classic diffusion of responsibility — everyone assumed someone else had verified the numbers."

**[THE TERRA EXPOSURE — 2:30–3:30]**
"When Terra/LUNA collapsed in May 2022 — wiping out $60 billion in value over four days — 3AC was catastrophically exposed. They had invested over $200 million in LUNA, which went to near-zero in 72 hours. But LUNA was just one wound. 3AC had leveraged positions across the entire crypto market. As prices fell everywhere, their collateral was being liquidated simultaneously from every direction.

Margin calls started arriving — from Voyager, Genesis, BlockFi, all at once. 3AC needed to either post more collateral or repay loans immediately. They had neither. And here's where it gets truly bizarre: Su Zhu and Kyle Davies simply stopped answering their phones. They stopped responding to emails. They stopped replying to Telegram messages. Lenders who were owed hundreds of millions of dollars were being left on read."

**[THE GHOST FOUNDERS — 3:30–4:30]**
"By late June 2022, lenders started going public. Voyager Digital disclosed a $650 million exposure to 3AC — and later filed for bankruptcy itself. Genesis Capital disclosed $2.36 billion in claims. BlockFi, facing its own liquidity crisis, was forced into a fire-sale deal with FTX — which, as you may recall from a previous episode, also later collapsed. The entire industry was a set of falling dominoes.

On June 27, a British Virgin Islands court ordered 3AC into liquidation. Bankruptcy liquidators were appointed and tasked with one primary job: find Su Zhu and Kyle Davies. They couldn't. The two founders had left Singapore. They were not responding to court-ordered notices. They were not appearing for required depositions. The liquidators literally could not locate the men whose names were on hundreds of billions in IOUs."

**[THE YACHT — 4:30–5:15]**
"And then came the detail that made this story immortal. It emerged that while 3AC was collapsing — while lenders were losing everything — Su Zhu and Kyle Davies had placed an order for a $50 million luxury superyacht. Named 'Much Wow' — a reference to the Doge meme. The order had been placed as the fund was imploding. The yacht became the symbol of everything wrong with crypto's boom era: borrowed money, zero accountability, and a Dogecoin meme as a vessel name. Su Zhu was eventually arrested in Singapore in September 2023 trying to board a flight. He served prison time. Kyle Davies gave the occasional interview from an undisclosed tropical location. Outstanding."

**[LESSONS — 5:15–6:00]**
"3AC is a textbook case of how reputation substitutes for regulation in unregulated markets. Every lender assumed someone else had done the homework. Every lender believed 3AC was too connected, too respected, too big to be reckless. Nobody had the complete picture of total exposure — because nobody asked for it. The total leverage was invisible until it wasn't.

The lesson: in any market — crypto or otherwise — reputation is not a financial statement. Counterparty risk means asking: if this goes wrong for everyone simultaneously, what is the actual collateral? If the answer is 'their name,' you've already lost."

**[OUTRO — 6:00–6:30]**
"$18 billion managed. $3.5 billion in lender claims. A $50 million yacht named after a Doge meme, ordered mid-collapse. And founders who were physically unfindable when the bills came due. Subscribe — because this story has at least twenty-seven cousins we've documented for you."
