## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,000 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"At 3pm and again at 9pm every day in 2018, millions of people all over the world stopped what they were doing. Opened an app. Waited for a host to appear live on their phone. And competed to answer 12 increasingly difficult trivia questions for a chance to win real cash prizes split among the winners. HQ Trivia had 2.38 million simultaneous players on a single day in January 2018 — a mobile record at the time. It was one of the most genuinely novel products of the smartphone era. And 18 months later, it was gone. Do you remember the last time you played?"

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"HQ Trivia was created by Rus Yusupov and Colin Kroll — the same two founders who had built Vine, the six-second video app that Twitter had acquired and then killed. After Vine's death, they took the lessons of real-time mobile engagement and applied them to something completely new: a live game show broadcast twice a day on your phone. A real human host. Real-time questions. Real money prizes for anyone who got all 12 right.

It launched in August 2017 and grew explosively through word of mouth. Scott Rogowsky — 'Quiz Daddy' — became an internet celebrity. Demi Lovato guest-hosted. The Oscars ran a dedicated HQ game. Tech Twitter discussed it constantly. The app was genuinely addictive. The 3pm and 9pm game times started to organize people's days in a way that felt, briefly, like a collective cultural ritual."

**[THE PEAK — 1:30–2:15]**
"January 14, 2018 was Super Bowl eve — the peak of American sports culture. On that day, 2.38 million people played HQ Trivia simultaneously. For context: at that moment, HQ Trivia had more simultaneous live users than most cable news networks had viewers. The app was valued at $100 million. VC firms were pitching to invest. Every major media company was asking whether they needed a live trivia product. Snapchat launched a copy called Snap Games. YouTube launched a copy. The format was being called the future of interactive entertainment."

**[THE PROBLEMS — 2:15–3:15]**
"But HQ Trivia had serious structural problems that no amount of hype could fix indefinitely. The prize pool was typically $2,500 to $5,000 per game, split among whoever answered all 12 correctly. With hundreds or thousands of correct players on big nights, individual winners received pennies. Not the incentive to build a loyal, returning audience on.

The app crashed regularly during peak games — and in a live product, a server crash at question 8 of 12 is not a minor annoyance. It destroys the experience and generates resentment. Aggressive question difficulty spikes were used to cull winners but felt unfair to regular players. And the content format was extremely hard to evolve — the core loop stayed the same from launch, and novelty eventually wore off.

Then in December 2018, Colin Kroll — co-founder and CEO — died of an accidental drug overdose. The company lost its leader at the most critical moment of its lifecycle."

**[THE DECLINE — 3:15–4:00]**
"Through 2019, HQ Trivia's numbers declined steadily. The company experimented with sports trivia, word games, and a paid subscription tier, but none generated meaningful traction. The app opened to anyone regardless of invite in late 2018 — removing the exclusivity effect — but without replacing it with something that sustained engagement.

By early 2020, HQ Trivia had raised additional capital but was running out of both money and ideas. On February 14, 2020 — Valentine's Day — the company's backer Intermedia Labs announced that without finding a buyer, HQ Trivia would shut down. It shut down within days. Staff were informed and let go almost immediately."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"HQ Trivia built something genuinely new — a real-time shared cultural moment on mobile. But the mistake was conflating the novelty with a business. A mechanic that 2 million people will try once is not the same as a product that builds habitual, recurring engagement. Sustainable apps need reasons to come back even after the novelty is gone: social features, progression, deeper community, content evolution, or genuine utility.

HQ had none of these. It was a party that everyone attended once — and then remembered fondly but never went back to. The monetization model, which was based entirely on brand sponsorships during live games, was never large enough to build a company around."

**[OUTRO — 5:00–5:15]**
"2.38 million simultaneous players. The most exciting new app of 2018. Gone by Valentine's Day 2020. If you're feeling nostalgic — that's the entire story. Subscribe for more apps you once loved and barely remember."
