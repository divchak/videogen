## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,100 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Picture this. It's November 2022. You open your crypto app to check your balance — and the screen just loads. And loads. Then a message appears: 'Withdrawals are temporarily suspended.' Your $30,000 is in there. Your rent money. Your emergency fund. Your trust. And the company holding it — the world's second-largest crypto exchange, valued at $32 billion — has just collapsed. Not in a year. Not in a month. In 72 hours. This is FTX. And yes — it gets worse."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Sam Bankman-Fried — known everywhere as SBF — was not your typical billionaire. He didn't wear tailored suits or live in a penthouse. He drove a battered Toyota Corolla. He slept on a beanbag at the office — literally. His apartment in the Bahamas was shared with roommates. His philosophy? Something called 'Effective Altruism' — earn as much money as humanly possible, then give it all away to save the world. It was noble. It was quotable. And the media could not get enough of it.

By 2021, FTX was processing over $15 billion in daily trades. SBF was on the cover of Forbes. He was on the cover of Fortune. Net worth estimated at $26 billion. He testified before the US Congress as the 'responsible face of crypto' — politely, clearly, in the same rumpled hoodie he wore everywhere. Politicians posed for photos with him. He donated to both parties — covering all bases, as one does. Wall Street loved him. Washington loved him. Everyone loved him. They all got burned."

**[THE CRACKS APPEAR — 1:30–2:45]**
"Now here's where the story starts unraveling — and it starts with a single article. On November 2nd, 2022, crypto news site CoinDesk published a report revealing that Alameda Research — SBF's private hedge fund — was holding most of its assets in FTT. Which was FTX's own token. Which FTX itself had created. Out of nothing. Think about that: the primary collateral backing SBF's entire empire was his own made-up coin. That's like claiming your net worth is $10 million and, when asked for proof, you hand someone a coupon book you printed at home.

When rival exchange Binance — which held $580 million worth of FTT — saw that report, CEO CZ posted exactly one tweet: 'We're selling our FTT.' That's it. One tweet. And it triggered a catastrophic bank run. Within 24 hours, FTX users tried to pull out $6 billion. The servers strained. Withdrawals slowed. Then they stopped entirely. The FTT token crashed 80% in two days. What had taken years to build took hours to destroy."

**[THE COLLAPSE — 2:45–4:00]**
"Binance swooped in with an offer to acquire FTX and bail everyone out. It lasted approximately one afternoon. When Binance's team looked at FTX's actual internal books — the real numbers, not the press release version — they found an $8 billion hole. Eight. Billion. Dollars. Unaccounted for. Gone. Binance walked away by evening, calling it 'beyond our ability to help.' Outstanding.

Now SBF was texting everyone. VCs, rival executives, sovereign wealth funds, hedge funds — anyone whose number he had. He was reportedly offering equity, promising fixes, begging for emergency capital. The replies? Silence. On November 11, 2022 — just nine days after that first CoinDesk article — FTX filed for bankruptcy. SBF resigned. Over one million customers found their accounts locked.

What investigators then uncovered was the real story: FTX had been secretly funneling customer deposits directly into Alameda Research to fund wild leveraged bets, political donations, $300 million worth of Bahamas real estate, and Super Bowl ads featuring Tom Brady and Larry David — who famously said in that commercial, 'Eh, I don't think so.' Larry David was, in fact, correct."

**[AFTERMATH & TRIAL — 4:00–5:15]**
"SBF was arrested in the Bahamas in December 2022. The US extradited him and he went to trial in October 2023 — one of the most watched financial trials in years. Former colleagues took the stand one by one. His ex-girlfriend Caroline Ellison, who ran Alameda Research, cooperated with prosecutors and testified in detail — describing spreadsheets, hidden balance sheets, and meetings where fraud was discussed as casually as choosing a lunch spot.

In November 2023, the jury found Sam Bankman-Fried guilty on all seven counts of fraud and conspiracy. Every. Single. Count. In March 2024 — at age 32 — he was sentenced to 25 years in federal prison. The customer losses? Estimated between $8 and $10 billion. The bankruptcy process has clawed back significant funds — creditors are expected to receive close to full dollar recovery thanks to rising crypto prices. But for the families who sold assets, missed payments, and had panic attacks over locked screens — no recovery check fully undoes that."

**[LESSON — 5:15–6:00]**
"Here's the uncomfortable truth this story leaves us with: the fraud was hiding in plain sight, and nobody asked hard questions because everyone was too busy being charmed. SBF's hoodie was a costume. The beanbag was a prop. The Toyota Corolla was brand strategy. The 'give-it-all-away' philosophy was a story that made people skip the audit.

The lesson: charisma is not audited. Media coverage is not due diligence. And if someone's financial structure seems deliberately complex — if the answer to 'how does this actually work?' is always one level deeper — that complexity might not be sophistication. It might be designed specifically to hide something. Always ask: where is the actual money? Who's auditing the books? Is any of this regulated?"

**[OUTRO — 6:00–6:30]**
"$32 billion. One exchange. 72 hours. A beanbag, a Toyota Corolla, and a hoodie that somehow fooled Wall Street, Washington, and the world's most sophisticated investors — all at once. If you think this kind of story is ancient history — we have 29 more episodes that suggest otherwise. Subscribe. We cover these collapses before they end up in textbooks."
