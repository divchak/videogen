## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,000 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"IRL — which stood for 'In Real Life' — was a social networking app that claimed to have 20 million monthly active users. Investors including SoftBank's Vision Fund valued it at $1.17 billion. A billion-dollar social app. And then, when the company shut down in June 2023, an SEC investigation found that 95% of those 20 million users were bots, automated accounts, or artificial activity. Not 5% fake. Not 20% inflated. Ninety-five percent. The social app called 'In Real Life' had almost no real users. And the story of how that was both possible — and undetected — is a cautionary tale that every investor in the digital economy needs to understand."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"IRL was founded in 2017 by Abraham Shafi, who positioned it as a social discovery app for real-world activities and events — a way to find out what your friends were actually doing and join them in person. The timing felt culturally relevant: there was growing anxiety about social media making people more isolated, more depressed, more passive. IRL's message was different: use this app to get off your phone and into the world. The name was the brand promise.

The app evolved to include group messaging, event creation, and community features. It targeted Gen Z and younger millennial users. It raised $16 million in 2019. Then, during the COVID pandemic — when virtually everyone was on their phones constantly and social apps were growing explosively — IRL raised $170 million in 2021, with SoftBank's Vision Fund leading at a $1.17 billion valuation."

**[THE BOT PROBLEM — 1:30–2:30]**
"The SEC investigation that followed the company's 2023 shutdown found something remarkable in its specificity: approximately 95% of IRL's reported user activity was not generated by real human beings. It was generated by automated bot accounts that mimicked human behavior patterns with sufficient sophistication to pass casual inspection during the due diligence process.

The genuine human user count — the actual people using the app — was closer to 1 million, not 20 million. The company had been reporting inflated metrics to investors throughout the funding and valuation process. The entire $1.17 billion valuation was premised on a social network at scale. The social network at scale was almost entirely artificial. In startup terms, the most important single number — monthly active users for a social app — was fabricated."

**[HOW DID THIS PASS DUE DILIGENCE? — 2:30–3:15]**
"This is the question that the Ozy Media and IRL stories both raise: how did serious institutional investors with large due diligence teams miss a 95% bot rate? The answer involves how metrics are typically verified in consumer app investing. App store download data is third-party verified. But monthly active users — the key metric — is typically reported by the company and accepted on trust, supplemented by pattern analysis that looks for anomalies.

IRL apparently used bots that behaved like real users — consistent session lengths, organic-seeming engagement patterns, activity spread across hours of the day. Without deeply technical forensic analysis specifically looking for artificial users, these patterns can pass casual review. The lesson: the most important metric for a consumer app is also the most manipulable. Independent verification is not optional."

**[THE SHUTDOWN — 3:15–4:00]**
"In June 2023, IRL abruptly shut down and laid off its staff. The company cited 'regulatory issues' as the reason — a phrase chosen presumably because 'we are under SEC investigation for securities fraud' was slightly more alarming. Abraham Shafi departed. The SEC launched a formal investigation.

The SEC's findings were eventually published: IRL had materially misled investors about the size and engagement of its user base. The case joined a growing body of precedent around digital metric fraud — alongside Ozy Media, alongside the numerous other cases where startup valuations were built on user numbers that weren't what they appeared. The app that was supposed to get people together in real life couldn't get real people into the app."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"IRL's collapse delivers a lesson about what makes digital fraud uniquely dangerous: unlike financial fraud, where money either exists in a bank account or it doesn't, digital user metrics exist on a spectrum from genuinely organic to entirely artificial — and the technology to inflate them is widely available and relatively inexpensive. For every FTX, there are dozens of smaller companies inflating user counts, engagement rates, and growth figures because the incentives — high valuations, generous terms, continued investment — reward the numbers, not the reality.

The specific vulnerability: social apps are valued almost entirely on user metrics. User metrics can be manufactured. The investor verification process rarely goes deep enough to catch sophisticated manipulation. And the regulatory framework for digital metric disclosure is still catching up to the opportunity for abuse."

**[OUTRO — 5:00–5:15]**
"$170 million raised. 20 million claimed users. 95% of them bots. The most ironic startup name in Silicon Valley history. Subscribe — because this story is still being written by every digital metrics dashboard that goes unverified."
