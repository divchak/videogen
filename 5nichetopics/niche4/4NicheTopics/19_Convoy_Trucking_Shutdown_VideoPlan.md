## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~950 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"On October 19, 2023, the CEO of Convoy sent a company-wide email. The message: the trucking marketplace startup — which had raised $900 million and was backed by Jeff Bezos, Bill Gates, and Marc Benioff — was shutting down in two weeks. Not two months. Two weeks. No restructuring plan. No sale process they could announce. No severance packages beyond what was legally required. Just: it's over, find new jobs. For hundreds of employees, this was the most expensive email they ever opened."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Convoy was founded in 2015 by Dan Lewis and Grant Goodale — both former Amazon executives — with a vision to digitize one of the most fragmented, inefficient industries in America: trucking. The US freight trucking market moves $800 billion worth of goods annually, and much of it runs through a web of phone calls, fax machines, and human brokers connecting shippers with available trucks.

Convoy's platform used algorithms to match shippers needing freight moved with independent truckers who had available capacity — in real time, with transparent pricing and digital tracking. The technology was real. The market was genuine. And the early traction was significant enough to attract Bezos, Gates, and Benioff as early individual investors, alongside institutional rounds that eventually totaled $900 million."

**[THE BUSINESS MODEL CHALLENGE — 1:30–2:30]**
"But digital freight brokerage is a commoditized, razor-margin business. The incumbents — C.H. Robinson, Echo Global Logistics, XPO — had decades of shipper relationships, established pricing power, and economies of scale. Startups like Uber Freight and Amazon Freight were also in the same market with enormous platform advantages. Convoy was competing against highly resourced players in a business where the core service — connecting a shipper to a truck — is ultimately a commodity.

To win market share, Convoy was taking on freight business at thin or negative margins — buying volume with price to grow the network. This is a classic growth strategy in software businesses where the marginal cost of an additional customer is near zero. In logistics, where every load has real operational costs, every below-market-price load is a real cash loss. The unit economics that work in SaaS don't transfer to physical freight."

**[MARKET COLLAPSE — 2:30–3:15]**
"The freight market had boomed during COVID-era supply chain disruptions — demand was through the roof, spot rates hit record highs, and Convoy's revenue grew explosively. Then came the correction. Through 2022 and into 2023, freight spot rates collapsed — falling 50 to 60 percent from their pandemic peaks as supply chains normalized and consumer spending shifted from goods back to services.

For Convoy, this was catastrophic timing. The revenue growth they'd built their financial model around reversed suddenly. The path to profitability — already narrow — became essentially invisible in a market where rates had been cut in half."

**[THE SHUTDOWN — 3:15–4:00]**
"In October 2023, facing an environment where both the freight market and the VC funding market for unprofitable logistics startups had dried up simultaneously, Convoy's leadership concluded there was no path forward. They reportedly explored potential acquirers and found no takers at a price that would make sense. On October 19, the company email went out. Two weeks to close. The Convoy name, the technology, and some assets were eventually acquired — but the company as an operating entity was gone.

The two-week notice was particularly noted across the startup community as unusually abrupt. People had mortgages, families, H-1B visa statuses dependent on employment. Two weeks is the minimum notice, not a thoughtful transition plan."

**[LESSONS — 4:00–4:50]**
"Convoy teaches a specific lesson about the limits of SaaS growth playbooks in physical industries. In software, you can grow at a loss and improve margins over time as the product scales — because marginal costs trend toward zero. In logistics, every incremental unit of business has real, physical costs. Growing a freight business at below-cost prices doesn't build a competitive moat. It builds a cash crisis. The combination of structural unit economics problems and a cyclical market downturn left no exit."

**[OUTRO — 4:50–5:05]**
"$900 million. Bezos, Gates, and Benioff. Two weeks notice. Subscribe — every week we document what the growth numbers don't tell you about the businesses underneath."
