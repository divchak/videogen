## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,100 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Here's a thought experiment. Take a company that rents desk space. Give it motivational posters, free kombucha, and a founder who tells employees they're part of a global consciousness movement. Call it a technology company. Watch as Wall Street and Silicon Valley value it at $47 billion. Then watch as — right before the IPO — someone actually reads the financial documents and the whole thing unravels in real time. This is WeWork. And the strangest part? The founder walked away with $1.7 billion."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"WeWork was founded in 2010 by Adam Neumann and Miguel McKelvey. The actual product was genuinely useful: co-working spaces. Rent a large floor, sub-divide it into flexible offices and open desk areas, add nice design, free coffee and beer, community events, and a mobile app — then lease those spaces to startups, freelancers, and growing companies who didn't want the burden of a traditional office lease. It was real. It worked. People liked it.

But Adam Neumann had bigger ambitions than running a co-working business. He wanted to be Steve Jobs. He started calling WeWork a 'physical social network.' A 'technology company.' He spoke at company events about living forever and becoming the world's first trillionaire. He talked about 'elevating the world's consciousness.' His employees gave him standing ovations. SoftBank's Masayoshi Son visited WeWork's New York headquarters and — in a 12-minute tour — committed $4.4 billion. Twelve minutes. SoftBank would eventually pour $18.5 billion total into this desk-rental company."

**[SOFTBANK AND THE $47B VALUATION — 1:30–2:30]**
"With SoftBank's money came the valuation that made accountants worldwide put down their coffee: $47 billion. WeWork was now the most valuable startup in America. For reference, that was more than Ford Motor Company. For a company that — let's be clear — rented desks. Neumann used the money to expand into 111 cities across 29 countries. He also used it to buy a $60 million private Gulfstream jet, billed to the company. He owned five personal properties. And then — in what is possibly the most brazenly self-dealing act in modern corporate history — he trademarked the word 'We' and sold that trademark to his own company for $5.9 million. The board eventually forced him to return it. There was, presumably, a very awkward board meeting."

**[THE S-1 — THE BEGINNING OF THE END — 2:30–3:30]**
"To go public, WeWork had to file an S-1 — a public prospectus disclosing their actual financials. And when those documents appeared in August 2019, the reaction from Wall Street was something between laughter and horror. WeWork had lost $1.6 billion in just the first six months of 2019 alone. Its lease obligations — the money it had committed to pay in future rent — exceeded $47 billion, equal to its entire claimed valuation. Revenue was $1.5 billion. There was no plausible path to profitability on any visible timeline.

The S-1 also revealed Neumann's self-dealing: he personally owned buildings that WeWork then rented from him. At a premium. He had borrowed hundreds of millions against his WeWork equity while the company itself was losing billions. Analysts began saying what had been obvious from outside for years: WeWork was a real estate company, with a marketing department and a very expensive candle."

**[THE OUSTER — 3:30–4:15]**
"The IPO collapsed. WeWork's valuation was slashed from $47 billion to somewhere under $10 billion in weeks. The company's board, under enormous investor pressure, finally did what they should have done years earlier: they pushed Adam Neumann out. He resigned in September 2019.

Then came the exit package. SoftBank — as part of the bailout — paid Neumann $1.7 billion to go away. That included $185 million in consulting fees for consulting no one ever asked for, and $500 million to buy out his voting shares. The man who ran his company to the edge of bankruptcy was paid $1.7 billion to leave. His employees — many of whom had taken below-market salaries in exchange for equity that was now nearly worthless — received almost nothing. The lesson in structural incentives was not subtle."

**[BANKRUPTCY — 4:15–5:00]**
"WeWork muddled along without Neumann, surviving through a series of restructurings. Then COVID-19 arrived in 2020 and emptied office buildings globally. Revenue collapsed. The company restructured again, and again. In November 2023 — four years after the failed IPO — WeWork filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. The company that SoftBank once valued at $47 billion could no longer pay its own rent. Let that sentence sit for a moment. A company that rents space to others — could not pay the rent on the space it was renting to rent."

**[NEUMANN'S RETURN — 5:00–5:30]**
"In a final act of Silicon Valley audacity, in 2022 Adam Neumann raised $350 million from Andreessen Horowitz — one of the most prestigious VC firms in the world — to launch a new real estate venture called Flow. The pitch: basically WeWork, but for apartment buildings. Andreessen Horowitz called it the 'largest initial check' they'd ever written to a single founder. The world did not seem to learn the lesson."

**[LESSONS — 5:30–6:15]**
"WeWork is the case study that every business school teaches about the danger of storytelling without substance. The narrative — 'we are a tech company, we are changing consciousness, we are the future' — was so compelling that it short-circuited basic analysis. Nobody asked the simple question: if WeWork's leases cost more than WeWork's revenue, how does this business work? The answer: it doesn't. It never did. The narrative just ran far ahead of the reality — and collected $18.5 billion along the way."

**[OUTRO — 6:15–6:30]**
"$47 billion. Free beer. A trademarked word. A $1.7 billion exit for the man who broke the company. And a new $350 million check 18 months later. Subscribe — we cover the stories that somehow keep getting funded."
