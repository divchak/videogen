## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~1,000 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"Olive AI was once the most valuable health-tech startup in America. It promised to automate the most tedious and costly administrative tasks in hospitals — insurance prior authorizations, claims processing, patient eligibility verification — using artificial intelligence. It was valued at $4 billion, claimed to serve 900 hospitals, and raised $900 million from serious investors. And then, without a dramatic collapse, without a fraud revelation, without a single shocking announcement — it quietly dismantled itself. Division by division. Layoff by layoff. Until almost nothing remained. This is what a $4 billion company looks like when it slowly admits it couldn't do what it said it could."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Olive AI was founded in 2012 by Sean Lane in Columbus, Ohio. The problem he was targeting was real and enormous: US hospitals spend over $1 trillion annually on administrative costs — billing, authorization, verification, claims — work that is fundamentally repetitive, rule-based, and ripe for automation. Olive used robotic process automation — software bots that replicate human computer interactions — to perform these tasks automatically, freeing up clinical staff for patient care.

The pitch resonated deeply. Healthcare administrators understood the problem intuitively. The technology, in demos, was impressive. By 2021, Olive had raised $400 million in a single round, pushing its valuation to $4 billion. Hundreds of hospital systems were customers. Sean Lane was a compelling speaker who positioned Olive at the frontier of where AI and healthcare intersect. The story was coherent, the problem was real, and the investors were serious."

**[THE PERFORMANCE QUESTIONS — 1:30–2:30]**
"But healthcare AI automation is significantly harder in practice than in a pitch deck. Hospital IT environments are not clean, modern tech stacks — they are decades of accumulated legacy systems from dozens of different vendors, patched together with integrations that were never designed to talk to each other. Deploying automation into these environments required complex, custom integration work that was expensive, slow, and often needed significant human oversight to function reliably.

The gap between what Olive's technology could do in a controlled demonstration and what it could reliably deliver in a live hospital environment was wide. Former employees and hospital partners, speaking to healthcare trade publications, described automation that required constant monitoring and intervention — which fundamentally undermined the cost-savings proposition. If you need someone watching the bot, the bot hasn't replaced the person."

**[THE SELF-DISMANTLING — 2:30–3:15]**
"Starting in 2022, Olive began making decisions that told the story without saying it. It sold its pharmacy automation unit. It sold its clinical documentation unit. It laid off large portions of its workforce — cutting from over 900 employees down through the hundreds. Each divestiture was framed in press releases as a 'strategic focus' or 'sharpening our core.' But when a company sells off its arms, its legs, and its torso in sequential quarters — that's not focus. That's a controlled unwinding.

By late 2023, Olive AI had sold or shut down most of its operations. The entity that remained was a fraction of the company that had claimed a $4 billion valuation."

**[LESSONS — 3:15–4:00]**
"Olive AI is a case study in the specific difficulties of healthcare technology that investors consistently underestimate. One: healthcare operates under strict compliance regimes that slow everything. HIPAA, security requirements, payer contracts, clinical workflow rules — all add friction that doesn't exist in most SaaS markets. Two: healthcare IT infrastructure is among the most fragmented and legacy-heavy in any industry. Three: the actual buyer — the hospital administrator — is deeply risk-averse, because the consequences of a broken automation are delayed care, billing errors, and audit exposure.

The addressable market was real. The technology was real. The business model required execution precision that healthcare's operational environment made extremely difficult to achieve at the scale needed to justify $4 billion."

**[OUTRO — 4:00–4:15]**
"$4 billion. 900 hospitals. $900 million raised. And a quiet dismantling that barely made headlines. Subscribe — the silent failures teach just as much as the dramatic ones."
