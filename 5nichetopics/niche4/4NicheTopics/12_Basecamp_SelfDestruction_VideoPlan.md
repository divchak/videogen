## a) 🎬 YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Full Narration ~950 words)

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:30]**
"On April 26, 2021, Basecamp's two founders — who had spent 20 years writing books about healthy workplace culture — sent their employees an internal memo. By the end of that week, roughly one third of the company had publicly resigned. The memo banned political discussions at work. The irony? The founders continued making politically charged posts on social media the entire time. This is the story of how the people who wrote the rulebook for good company culture became the cautionary tale."

**[BACKGROUND — 0:30–1:30]**
"Jason Fried and David Heinemeier Hansson — DHH — founded Basecamp in 1999. For two decades, they were among the most admired voices in the startup world. Their books — 'Getting Real,' 'Rework,' and 'Remote' — sold millions of copies and genuinely shaped how a generation of founders thought about work. Strong opinions. Anti-hustle. Pro-balance. Anti-unnecessary meetings. They were the counterculture within startup culture.

Their product — Basecamp, a project management tool — was successful, profitable, and used by millions. They didn't need venture capital. They were bootstrapped and proud of it. They wrote and spoke constantly about values, transparency, and treating employees as humans. DHH in particular was prolific on Twitter, opinionated on everything from software design to political economy. They had built, over 20 years, enormous credibility. They spent it in about 72 hours."

**[THE MEMO — 1:30–2:30]**
"The April 2021 memo, published publicly by DHH and Fried on the Basecamp blog, announced five significant policy changes. One: no more political discussions on company communication platforms. Two: the company would stop forming committees around diversity, equity, inclusion, and other 'societal issues.' Three: the popular 360-degree peer review process would be eliminated. Four: certain employee benefits — fitness stipends, education allowances, and a few others — were being cut. Five: no more 'paternalistic' benefits.

The context behind the memo leaked quickly: Basecamp had an internal list called 'funny customer names' that had been circulating among employees for years. The list contained names that employees of color found racially insensitive. When employees raised concerns about it, the response from leadership was — effectively — to ban the kind of discussions that had surfaced the concern. The memo wasn't presented as a response to the names list. But the timing was not ambiguous to the people inside the company."

**[THE WALKOUT — 2:30–3:15]**
"Basecamp had approximately 57 employees. In the days after the memo, roughly 20 of them — over one third — announced their resignations publicly, most via Twitter or personal blog posts. Many wrote detailed explanations of why they were leaving. The posts described a disconnect between the company's stated values and the decisions being made by leadership. The mass public resignation from a company of 57 people became international tech news.

DHH and Fried responded publicly with blog posts defending the memo and their reasoning. They doubled down rather than walked back. The discourse became a flashpoint in a broader conversation about politics in workplaces — but the specific context of the names list, and the specific timing of the memo, left the founders' position looking less like principled policy and more like a silencing of legitimate concerns."

**[THE PRODUCT FALLOUT — 3:15–4:00]**
"The controversy landed at the worst possible time. Basecamp was in the middle of launching Hey — a paid email service they'd been working on for years, positioning as a thoughtful, values-driven alternative to Gmail. Hey launched amid the public meltdown. Potential users who had been considering it found themselves reading about the internal culture implosion instead of the product features. The reputational damage was real.

More broadly: Basecamp had built its brand explicitly on culture and values. Their books, their talks, their blog posts — all of it positioned them as the company that did things right. When the culture broke publicly, the brand paid the price. Companies that don't build their identity around culture can survive cultural problems. Companies that sell culture as their differentiator cannot."

**[AFTERMATH — 4:00–4:45]**
"The company continued operating. Hey continued operating. DHH became increasingly active and outspoken politically on Twitter and later Substack — the precise kind of behavior the memo had cited as unhealthy when done by employees. The irony was noted, frequently, by many people.

In 2022, DHH co-founded a new company — 37signals became two products rather than one. He continued writing and speaking. But the position Basecamp had held — as the gold standard for how to run a thoughtful software company — was gone. Once-unconditional admirers became critics. The credibility that took 20 years to build had a very specific expiration date."

**[LESSONS — 4:45–5:30]**
"Basecamp's story is about what happens when a company's behavior contradicts its identity. You can have whatever workplace policies you want — and there are legitimate arguments for keeping certain discussions out of the workplace. But when you build your entire brand around values and culture, you are held to a higher standard than other companies. When your own founders are publicly outspoken on political topics while banning employees from discussing them — the contradiction is not lost on people. Credibility is expensive to build and fast to spend."

**[OUTRO — 5:30–5:50]**
"One memo. Twenty resignations. Twenty years of credibility — spent. And a founders' Twitter feed that kept right on going. Subscribe — we document the gap between what companies say and what they do."
